TITLE: Total RNA and MicroRNA Transcriptomic Responses of Human Retinal Müller Glial Cells to Infection with Toxoplasma gondii Tachyzoites
PMID: 41693810
PMC: 
------------------------------------------------------------

# ABSTRACT
Purpose Ocular toxoplasmosis, caused by infection with Toxoplasma gondii , is characterized by retinal necrosis and reactive intraocular inflammation. Müller glial cells are a principal retinal host cell population for T. gondii . The goal of this research was to delineate potential involvements of Müller glial cells in ocular toxoplasmosis at a molecular level. Design Laboratory-based study. Samples Human retinal Müller glial cells infected with T. gondii plus noninfected cells. Methods Monolayers of Müller glial cells isolated from human retina (6 donor eye pairs) were infected for 24 hours with GT1 or GPHT strain T. gondii tachyzoites (multiplicity of infection of 5), or incubated in parallel without infection. Total RNA and small RNA were extracted from cell monolayers, sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 and NextSeq 550 platforms, respectively, and aligned to GRCh38. Transcriptomic responses to infection with each strain were compared for differential expression (false discovery rate <5% and twofold change). These data were interrogated for enrichment of Reactome and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways, and Gene Ontology in InnateDB; putative transcription factor binding sites in HOMER; and potential microRNA–mRNA interactions in multiMiR. Main Outcome Measures Total and small RNA transcriptomes. Results 6.3% of total RNA and 2.4% of small RNA changed in GT1-infected cells (582 upregulated and 210 downregulated transcripts and 20 upregulated microRNAs) versus 4.3% of total RNA and 1.5% of small RNA in GPHT-infected cells (400 upregulated and 137 downregulated transcripts and 12 upregulated microRNAs). Seventy-six transcripts and 4 microRNAs were different between strains; most were increased by both, but GT1 induced higher levels than GPHT. Enriched pathways and ontologies were dominated by DNA replication and intracellular metabolic activities, and the immune response for GT1 and GPHT. Seven of 8 transcription factor binding sites were shared for GT1 and GPHT infections, all overexpressed, including sites for p65/RELA and E2F family members. Across the strains, miR-18a-5p was the most connected microRNA in predicted mRNA target networks. Conclusions This work demonstrates that human retinal Müller glial cells shift to a proliferative and inflammatory phenotype when infected with T. gondii tachyzoites, consistent with a central role in the characteristic pathology of ocular toxoplasmosis. # BODY
## Methods
Study Design Monolayers of 6 primary human retinal Müller glial cell isolates were separately infected for 24 hours with GT1 or GPHT strain T. gondii tachyzoites (multiplicity of infection of 5) or incubated in parallel without infection. Total RNA and small RNA were extracted from the Müller cell monolayers and subsequently sequenced up to 40 million and 22 million reads per sample, respectively. Reads were aligned to the human GRCh38 reference sequence. After adjusting for donor effect, genes differentially expressed between GT1 or GPHT strain T. gondii –infected and noninfected Müller cells were identified and assessed for enrichment of molecular pathways and ontology. Differential gene expression data were explored further by transcription factor binding site prediction and microRNA (miRNA)–mRNA interaction analyses. The raw data generated in this work are available in the Gene Expression Omnibus repository of the National Center for Biotechnology Information under GSE145836 . Human Retinal Müller Glial Cell Isolation, Culture, and Phenotyping Human cadaver donor eyes were sourced from the Eye Bank of South Australia (Adelaide, Australia). Retina was dissected from donor eye pairs and digested in 0.5 mg/mL of Dispase II (Thermo Fisher Scientific-Gibco) in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) with 2% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (FBS; Thermo Fisher Scientific-Gibco) for 10 minutes at 37 °C. The method of cell isolation has been reported in detail previously. 20 In brief, the digested tissue was dissociated by pipetting, passed through a 30 μm-pore strainer, and pelleted by centrifugation. The pelleted cells were resuspended and immediately plated in 10 cm diameter dishes (growth area, 60 cm 2 ) in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (catalog number: 11965), 1× Non-Essential Amino Acids Solution, 1× GlutaMAX Supplement, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 100 U/mL penicillin-100 μg/mL streptomycin, and 10% FBS (all sourced from Thermo Fisher Scientific-Gibco) and incubated at 37 °C and 5% CO 2 in air. Half the medium was changed after the cells had been cultured for 5 days, and then once weekly. At confluency, the cells were stored frozen in liquid nitrogen ahead of use in the T. gondii infection studies. All Müller cell isolates were immunophenotyped for expression of human retinal Müller glial cell markers: cellular retinaldehyde binding protein (CRALBP), glutamine synthetase (GS), vimentin, and glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP). Cells monolayers in 96-well plates (growth area, 0.3 cm 2 ) were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 10 minutes, permeabilized with 0.2% Triton X-100 (Merck-Sigma Aldrich) in PBS for 5 minutes, and blocked for 1 hour with 2% bovine serum albumin (Merck-Sigma Aldrich) and 0.05% Triton X-100 in PBS. The fixed cell monolayers were incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary antibody in blocking solution, then for 1 hour at room temperature with the appropriate Alexa 488- or Alexa 594-conjugated secondary antibody (Thermo Fisher Scientific-Molecular Probes) at 1 μg/mL in blocking solution, and finally with 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (Merck-Sigma Aldrich) at 50 ng/mL in PBS for 2 minutes. Between each labelling step, the cell monolayers were washed thrice with 0.1% Tween 20 (Merck-Sigma Aldrich) in PBS for 5 minutes each. Details of the primary antibodies, including working concentrations, are provided in Table S1 (available at www.ophthalmologyscience.org ). Toxoplasma gondii Culture Two virulent T. gondii strains—GT1 (haplogroup 1, clonal) and GPHT (haplogroup 6, exotic) 21 , 22 —were the generous gift of Dr Jitender P. Dubey (US Department of Agriculture, Beltsville, MD) and Dr L. David Sibley (Washington University, St Louis, MO). Both strains were maintained in tachyzoite form by serial passage in confluent human neonatal dermal fibroblast monolayers (Cascade Biologics) in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium supplemented with 1% FBS, at 37 °C and 5% CO 2 in air. Infection of Human Retinal Müller Glial Cells with Toxoplasma gondii Confluent monolayers of primary human retinal Müller glial cells in 6-well plates (growth area, 10 cm 2 ) were infected with freshly egressed GT1 or GPHT strain T. gondii tachyzoites (multiplicity of infection of 5) resuspended in Müller cell medium with FBS reduced to 5%, or treated with fresh medium alone, and incubated for 4 hours at 37 °C and 5% CO 2 in air. Subsequently, the cell monolayers were washed twice with new medium to remove extracellular tachyzoites, and the plates were returned to incubate for a total of 24 hours. Parasite viability was evaluated by human fibroblast monolayer plaque assay and required to be at least 15%, which is the expected viability of freshly egressed tachyzoites. RNA Extraction RNA was harvested from all Müller cell monolayers 24 hours after infection with TRIzol Reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific-Ambion), according to the manufacturer’s instructions, and stored frozen at –80°C. The concentration of the extracted RNA was determined by spectrophotometry on the Qubit 2.0 Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific-Invitrogen), and integrity was confirmed on the 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies). RNA Sequencing Total RNA and small RNA were converted to cDNA libraries using the TruSeq Stranded Total RNA Kit and the TruSeq Small RNA Library Prep Kit (Illumina), respectively, in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions with one exception: for small RNA, cDNA purification was performed using Pippin Prep (Sage Science). Sequencing was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 (Illumina, 150 cycles run as paired-end 2 × 150 bp) and NextSeq 550 (Illumina, 75 cycles run as single-read 1 × 75 bp) for total RNA and small RNA, respectively. The PhiX Control v3 (Illumina) was used as the sequencing control. Processing and Statistical Analysis of RNA Sequencing Data Fastq read quality was visualized in FastQC 23 v0.11.3 and summarized with MultiQC 24 v1.7. Cutadapt 25 v1.18 was applied to remove the small RNA adapter sequence. Sequences were trimmed with Trimmomatic 26 v0.38 for a minimum length of 50 bases and a maximum length of 125 bases (total RNA) or for a minimum length of 16 bases and a maximum length of 24 bases (miRNA), with a sliding window of Phred 25 over 4 bases. Before performing the alignment, RNA sequences were classified against the National Center for Biotechnology Information RefSeq database using Kraken 27 v2.0.7 to confirm human and T. gondii identities. Reads were aligned to the GRCh38 human genome with Ensembl v93 annotation using HISAT2 28 v2.1.0 on default alignment parameters (total RNA) or with bowtie2 29 on very-sensitive-local mode (miRNA). featureCounts 30 v1.5.0-p2 was used to count aligned reads. The data were then transferred into R v3.6.0 for analysis. Counts were normalized using the trimmed mean of M-values method in EdgeR 31 v3.26.5 prior to multidimensional scaling and differential gene expression analysis, performed by generalized linear model likelihood ratio test. Gene sets were filtered for at least 1 count per million in 10% of samples prior to differential expression analysis. Significant differences in gene expression were defined by a false discovery rate <5% and twofold change. For total RNA, Reactome and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways, and Gene Ontology overrepresentation analyses were carried out in InnateDB. 32 Drawing on the DrugBank 5.0 and KEGG Pathway 113.0 databases, NetworkAnalyst 33 v3.0 was employed to discover potential protein–drug interactions, which were visualized in Cytoscape 34 v.3.10.1. HOMER 35 v4.1 was used for the prediction of transcription factor binding sites enriched among differentially expressed gene sets. Putative miRNA targets were retrieved using multiMiR 36 R package v1.6.0. These were combined with a Pearson correlation analysis to identify gene sets targeted by each miRNA, and pathway and ontology enrichment analyses were performed on those gene sets. Validated interactions between upregulated miRNAs and downregulated mRNAs were queried in multiMiR R package v1.26.0, filtering for negative regulatory interactions, and miRNA–mRNA regulatory networks were plotted in Cytoscape. For both the total RNA and miRNA datasets, ComBat, implemented in the sva 37 R package v3.32.1, was used to correct for donor effect. ## Results
Six primary human retinal Müller glial cell isolates were prepared from paired eyecups of 3 women and 3 men, aged 43 to 69 years at death. The time from death to processing of the tissue averaged 33 hours. Immunolabelled isolates consistently expressed human retinal Müller glial cell markers: CRALBP, GS, vimentin, and GFAP. Cell monolayers were infected with freshly egressed GT1 or GPHT strain T. gondii tachyzoites or incubated in parallel without infection. Fibroblast monolayer plaque assays, conducted in sextuplicate with the same tachyzoite preparations used for these infections, indicated parasite viabilities >30% (range, 31.0%–61.5%), which was more than double the standard for robust cellular infections at a multiplicity of infection of 5. Measurements of percentage of reads classified as T. gondii and expression of T. gondii Hsp90 transcript, a marker for tachyzoite growth, 38 in RNA extracts from the infected cells indicated comparable infection loads across the 2 strains ( Fig S1 A, B, respectively, available at www.ophthalmologyscience.org ). The Müller cell response to infection with GT1 and GPHT strain T. gondii was interrogated by RNA-seq of total RNA and small RNA extracted from the 6 isolates 24 hours after infection with the parasite. Cross-referencing the total RNA transcriptome with bacterial, archeal, viral, and eukaryotic genomes revealed a mean 11.1% (range, 7.05%–15.12%) of reads in T. gondii –infected samples aligned to the parasite genome and no evidence for other contaminants ( Fig 1 A). For total RNA, there were 43.9 × 10 6 mean paired reads across the 18 samples; averaging the 6 noninfected samples, 99.3% of reads aligned to the human genome, and 59.1% were assigned to annotated genes. For small RNA, there were 18.7 × 10 6 mean paired reads across the 18 samples; averaging the noninfected samples, 99.7% of reads aligned to the human genome, and 57.9% were assigned to annotated genes. Alignment statistics for total RNA and small RNA are presented in Tables S2 and S3 (available at www.ophthalmologyscience.org ), respectively. Figure 1 A, Bar plot representing RNA composition of primary human retinal Müller glial cell isolates (1–6) 24 hours after infection with GT1 or GPHT strain Toxoplasma gondii tachyzoites or cultured in parallel without infection. B, C, Multidimensional scaling plots illustrating ( B ) total RNA transcriptome and ( C ) small RNA transcriptome of cells infected with GT1 strain or GPHT strain T. gondii tachyzoites, or noninfected control cells, pre- and post-adjustment for donor effect. On pre-adjustment plots, different shapes represent individual donor isolates. On postadjustment plots, circles indicate groupings based on infection status. PC = principal coordinate. Multidimensional scaling indicated that total RNA and small RNA transcriptomes clustered primarily by donor ( Fig 1 B and C [before adjustment], respectively). After adjustment for the donor effect, however, there was obvious separation of Müller cell gene expression according to T. gondii infection status (ie, GT1 strain-infected versus GPHT strain-infected versus noninfected) for both total RNA and small RNA ( Fig 1 B and C [after adjustment], respectively). Given the focus of this work on Müller cell responses to parasite infection, donor identification was incorporated into the implemented differential expression model. Differential expression analysis indicated greater gene expression changes in Müller cells after infection with GT1 strain T. gondii in comparison to GPHT strain. For total RNA, levels of 792 gene transcripts were significantly different between GT1 strain-infected and noninfected cells, including 582 that were increased, and 210 that were decreased ( Fig 2 A). Levels of 537 gene transcripts were significantly different between GPHT strain-infected and noninfected cells, including 400 that were increased and 137 that were decreased ( Fig 2 B). When GT1 strain-infected and GPHT strain-infected samples were compared directly, 76 genes were significantly differentially expressed; the majority of these genes were expressed at increased levels in both conditions relative to the noninfected control, but infection with GT1 strain tended to induce a higher level of change than GPHT strain, as exemplified by the top-ranked differentially expressed genes: TNFRSF9, LAMB3, TRAF1, REL, and FOXA1 ( Fig 2 C). Reactome and KEGG pathways, and Gene Ontology enrichment analyses were performed on the differentially expressed genes from the total RNA dataset and separately on the targets of the differentially expressed miRNAs from the small RNA dataset. For total RNA, although the specific descriptors varied for GT1 strain-infected versus GPHT strain-infected cells, overall, the pathways and ontologies enriched with upregulated genes were heavily dominated by DNA replication and intracellular metabolic activities for both conditions of infection ( Fig 5 A, B). Additionally, for both conditions, immune response-related pathways and groupings were highly active, particularly chemokine and cytokine activities. Among the mRNA targets of upregulated miRNAs, GT1 and GPHT strain infections modulated pathways and ontologies involved with intracellular protein processing ( Fig 6 A, B). Infection with the GPHT strain also highly modulated key growth factor-directed intracellular signaling. ## Discussion
A recent review of the cellular responses to infection with T. gondii has highlighted differences between different host cell populations. 40 Retinal Müller glial cells are activated in ocular toxoplasmosis, 17 and they present a key host cell population for T. gondii tachyzoites during an active infection. 12 We report the total RNA and small RNA transcriptomic responses of multiple primary human Müller cell isolates to infection with GT1 clonal strain and GPHT exotic strain tachyzoites. Numerically, the GT1 strain had a greater impact on Müller cells than the GPHT strain: defining differential gene expression stringently as twofold change and false discovery rate <5%, 6.3% of total RNA and 2.4% of small RNA changed in cells infected with the GT1 strain versus 4.3% of total RNA and 1.5% of small RNA with the GPHT strain. Qualitatively, transcriptomic responses were similar, as shown by the interstrain differential expression comparison, in which most genes were expressed at higher levels than those of control, albeit more highly in GT1 strain infection. Consistently, although specific pathway and ontology terms varied between GT1 and GPHT, there was enrichment in 2 main aspects of cell activity for both parasite strains: proliferation and the immune response. T. gondii has a population structure of clonal and atypical strains, and there is considerable interest in correlating genotype with parasite activity and clinical disease in humans. 41 Overall, we identified more similarities than differences in the response to infection with natural isolate clonal and exotic strains. Indeed, investigations by us and others have identified many strain-independent phenomena, even between strains considered virulent and avirulent, that might be expected to impact parasite activity and/or disease manifestations. For example, using an in vitro primary bovine endothelial cell model, Rojas-Barón et al 42 showed that S-phase arrest of the host cell cycle, mis-segregation of chromosomes, and failed cytokinesis all occurred similarly across virulent and avirulent clonal strains. As another example, Su et al 43 reported that the mir-185/ARAF axis modulated host cell apoptosis in T. gondii infection with different strains in vitro and in vivo. We have observed a common growth factor-mediated proliferative effect of different strains on primary retinal pigment epithelial cells. 44 Interestingly, our analysis of 19 clinical studies reveals that all strains—virulent and avirulent, clonal and atypical—cause significant ocular disease in humans. 2 As an obligate intracellular parasite, T. gondii is dependent on its host cell for metabolites plus organelles, such as mitochondria and lipid droplets, to support its replication. 45 In vitro studies indicate that human retinal Müller cells are highly supportive of parasite replication. 12 The characteristic full-thickness retinal involvement observed on OCT in patients is consistent with infection of Müller cells that extend from inner to outer retina. 46 Thus, it is logical that gene expression changes in infected Müller cells are dominated by cell cycle and DNA replicative processes apparent in the total RNA transcriptome enrichment analysis, and protein synthesis and ultrastructural growth-related processes seen in the miRNA target enrichment analysis. This proproliferative cell switching is also reflected in enrichment of E2F family member transcription factor binding motifs in upregulated gene transcripts in infected Müller cells, with key roles in the cell cycle. 47 , 48 Robust leukocytic infiltration of the posterior eye is characteristic of ocular toxoplasmosis, particularly involving the vitreous and producing the “headlight in the fog” sign that ophthalmologists use to diagnose the disease. 10 Results of the gene transcript enrichment analysis indicated a proinflammatory response of the Müller cells to infection, including production of multiple chemokines that promote leukocytic extravasation. This is consistent with findings of Kikumura et al, 49 who observed upregulation of numerous chemokines in the retina by real-time polymerase chain reaction array in a mouse ocular toxoplasmosis model. Knight et al, 50 working with the human MIO-M1 Müller cell line, hypothesized that Müller cells acted in an autocrine manner to contain parasite growth and were surprised by the inflammatory cytokine production of infected cells. Enrichment of Rel homology domain family member, p65 or RELA, in the transcription factor binding sites of enriched genes is consistent with a highly inflammatory cellular activity. 51 The most connected miRNA for the 2 T. gondii strains was miR-18a-5p: although not previously reported in the context of toxoplasmosis, miR-18a has been described in the retina, having both proinflammatory and proproliferative activities. 52 Our findings may have relevance for the approach to managing ocular toxoplasmosis in the clinic. Currently available antiparasitic drugs do not cure toxoplasmosis, and there is an ongoing, generally unpredictable risk of reactivation of toxoplasmic retinal lesions. 9 Drug resistance is reported, but ability of actively replicating tachyzoites to differentiate into dormant bradyzoites may be an even more important factor in the inability of antiparasitic drugs to eradicate the parasite from the tissue. 53 , 54 Tachyzoite-to-bradyzoite conversion may be triggered by various local conditions, including the production of certain proinflammatory molecules by host cells. 55 Our findings suggest a tailored anti-inflammatory strategy could limit this conversion in the retina, rendering the parasite more susceptible to antimicrobial drugs. Specifically, infection of human retinal Müller cell isolates with either GT1 or GPHT strains induced expression of interleukin-6 ( Table S4 ), a well-established conversion trigger. 56 A recombinant humanized anti-interleukin-6 monoclonal antibody formulated for intravitreal injection (vamikibart), is currently in clinical trials for patients with noninfectious uveitic macular edema. 57 We speculate that this biologic agent might block tachyzoite-to-bradyzoite conversion in the retina; however, any therapeutic value would have to be weighed against the potential for persisting tachyzoites to promote tissue damage through host cell lysis. Our work involved Müller cells that we isolated in-house from human retina. 20 We 58 and others 50 , 59 , 60 have used the MIO-M1 human retinal Müller glial cell line—described and generously shared widely by Dr Astrid Limb, Institute of Ophthalmology, University College London, London, United Kingdom 61 —to investigate molecular interactions between T. gondii and human retinal Müller cells. By applying single-cell RNA-seq to human adult retina, Lukowski et al 62 have shown that the molecular phenotype of MIO-M1 cells differs from that of primary Müller cells and has higher similarity with that of primary retinal astrocytes, and other authors have emphasized the need to undertake studies on mechanisms of ocular toxoplasmosis using primary human Müller cells. 50 We saw a strong donor effect in profiling the cells by RNA-seq, requiring statistical correction to focus on the effect of parasite infection. Aside of the expected interindividual differences, Müller cells undergo broad ultrastructural and molecular changes with age. Nag 63 noted clear differences between “lower age” and “aging” groups, differentiated at 60 years. Our donors were aged between 43 and 69 years at death. Cell phenotype is also impacted by pathology 51 ; none of the 6 human donors had a history of retinal disease. Finally, the location of a Müller cell within the retina also impacts its phenotype. 64 , 65 In our work, which used whole retinal isolates at early passage and thus a combination of posterior pole and peripheral cells, this is less likely to be an issue. In conclusion, we used RNA-seq to demonstrate the transcriptomic responses of human retinal Müller glial cells to infection with T. gondii . The investigation was necessarily limited to in vitro experiments but had strength in its use of multiple human primary cell isolates. We examined responses to one natural clonal strain and one natural exotic strain. Future research could apply our methods with additional clonal and exotic T. gondii strains, including laboratory-adapted strains. Our work shows that human retinal Müller glial cells shift to a proliferative and inflammatory phenotype when infected with T. gondii tachyzoites. This is consistent with a central role for these cells in both the retinal cell lysis and the inflammatory reaction that characterize ocular toxoplasmosis.
