TITLE: What Drosophila spermatocytes tell us about the mechanisms underlying cytokinesis
PMID: 22927345
PMC: 
------------------------------------------------------------

# ABSTRACT
Cytokinesis separates the genomic material and organelles of a dividing cell equitably into two physically distinct daughter cells at the end of cell division. This highly choreographed process involves coordinated reorganization and regulated action of the actin and microtubule cytoskeletal systems, an assortment of motor proteins, and membrane trafficking components. Due to their large size, the ease with which exquisite cytological analysis may be performed on them, and the availability of numerous mutants and other genetic tools, Drosophila spermatocytes have provided an excellent system for exploring the mechanistic basis for the temporally programmed and precise spatially localized events of cytokinesis. Mutants defective in male meiotic cytokinesis can be easily identified in forward genetic screens by the production of multinucleate spermatids. In addition, the weak spindle assembly checkpoint in spermatocytes, which causes only a small delay of anaphase onset in the presence of unattached chromosomes, allows investigation of whether gene products required for spindle assembly and chromosome segregation are also involved in cytokinesis. Perhaps due to the large size of spermatocytes and the requirement for two rapid-fire rounds of division without intervening S or growth phases during meiosis, male meiotic mutants have also revealed much about molecular mechanisms underlying new membrane addition during cytokinesis. Finally, cell type-specific differences in the events that set up and complete cytokinesis are emerging from comparison of spermatocytes with cells undergoing mitosis either elsewhere in the organism or in tissue culture.

# BODY
## Introduction
Cytokinesis, which partitions the cytoplasmic and nuclear content of the mother cell into two daughters at the end of cell division (reviewed in Barr and Gruneberg, 2007 ; Green et al., 2012), is both a fundamental task in normal development and is crucial for maintaining ploidy in adult tissues produced throughout life in stem cell lineages. In animal cells, a contractile ring composed of F-actin filaments and active Myosin II assembled on the inner face of the plasma membrane around the cell equator ( Schroeder, 1990 ; Miller, 2011 ) generates the constricting force that separates the cell into two. How the cell builds the contractile ring at just the right place to cleanly and reliably separate the genetic and cytoplasmic contents is both a key regulatory problem and a long-standing question in the field of cytokinesis. Constriction of the actomyosin ring draws the plasma membrane and the underlying cortex inward, generating a cleavage furrow. This furrow ingresses until the daughter cells remain connected by a thin cytoplasmic bridge, dubbed the midbody. During the last step of cytokinesis, abscission, the intercellular bridge is ultimately severed, leading to two fully-separated daughter cells. Cytokinesis is an intricate process involving at least one hundred proteins and requiring tight coordination between different cytoskeletal components and the plasma membrane ( Eggert et al., 2006 ; Barr and Gruneberg, 2007 ; Pollard, 2010 ; Green et al., 2012). Much of our knowledge regarding the molecular players and underlying mechanisms that regulate and drive cytokinesis derives from genetic dissection of this process in suitable model organisms, such as Drosophila melanogaster, Caenorhabditis elegans and Schizosaccharomyces pombe (reviewed in Giansanti et al., 2001 , Glotzer, 2005; Eggert et al., 2006 ; Bathe and Chang, 2010 ; Pollard and Wu, 2010 ; Green et al., 2012). In this review we describe the advantages offered by Drosophila spermatocytes for combined genetic and cytological studies of the mechanisms that underlie cytokinesis and examine the novel contributions from such studies to the field.


## Drosophila spermatocytes as a system for the study of cytokinesis
Drosophila spermatocytes offer a well-suited cell system for immunocytological and in vivo analysis of the structural and morphological events that underly cytokinesis, in part due to the large size of male meiotic cells and to the fact that clusters of primary and secondary spermatocytes undergo meiotic division in synchrony. This, coupled with the ease of identification of mutants that affect male meiosis by a simple screen of unfixed tissue and the many other genetic tools available in Drosophila , has led to a detailed descriptive and mechanistic understanding of both common and cell type-specific events of cytokinesis in animal cells. Male germ cells differentiate throughout reproductive life from adult stem cells. In Drosophila , when a male germ line stem cell located at the apical tip of the testis divides, one daughter normally self-renews and maintains stem cell identity, while the other daughter becomes enclosed in two somatic support cells and initiates differentiation as a gonialblast ( Fuller and Spradling, 2007 ). The gonialblast initiates four rounds of synchronous spermatogonial mitotic divisions to produce a cyst of 16 germ cells, which undergo premeiotic S phase and become spermatocytes. During the extended G2 phase of the cell cycle that is meiotic prophase, each spermatocyte undergoes impressive growth, leading to a 25-fold increase in volume ( Lindsley and Tokuyasu, 1980 ). As a result, by the onset of the first meiotic division, approximately 90 hours after premeiotic S phase, spermatocytes are considerably larger than most somatic cells. The 16 spermatocytes in the cyst then synchronously undergo the G2/M transition of meiosis I and embark in quick succession on the first then the second meiotic division, producing a cyst of 64 haploid round spermatids. Drosophila spermatogonia and spermatocytes, like male germ cells in mammals, undergo incomplete cytokinesis, both during the mitotic spermatogonial divisions and the two meiotic divisions. In both cases, cleavage furrows constrict, but abscission does not take place, and daughter cells remain interconnected by intercellular bridges called ring canals. Other than this developmentally-programmed lack of abscission, the mechanism of cleavage furrow formation and ingression appears largely conserved in spermatocytes compared with other animal cells ( Giansanti et al. 2001 and this review). During both the first and second meiotic divisions, the nuclear envelope does not disassemble by metaphase, but becomes fenestrated near the spindle poles. A small number of microtubules penetrate these fenestrations to contact the kinetochores, while the bulk of the metaphase spindle remains outside the nuclear envelope ( Tates, 1971 ; Church and Lin, 1982 ). From metaphase to telophase, nuclei become spindle-shaped and are surrounded by a system of 5 to 7 double membranes called parafusorial membranes. During each meiotic division, alignment of mitochondria along the pole-to-equator spindle microtubles outside of the parafusorial membranes ensures the equal partitioning of mitochondria between the two daughter cells at the end of cytokinesis. The nuclear envelopes eventually break down and new nuclear envelopes form around the separated daughter nuclei in telophase. After the second meiotic division, the mitochondria in each early round spermatid fuse to form a complex, interlaced structure called the nebenkern ( Fuller, 1993 ; Fig. 1A ). When viewed by phase-contrast optics, wild type early round spermatids contain a phase-dark nebenkern and a single phase-light haploid nucleus ( Fig. 1A–B ), and all the 64 interconnected spermatids within a cyst have the same sized nebenkern and the same sized nucleus. Mutants defective in meiotic cytokinesis can be easily identified by examining the pattern of nuclei and nebenkern in onion-stage spermatid cysts. Failure of cytokinesis during one or both meiotic divisions impairs the partitioning of mitochondria into separate daughter cells, and if segregation of the chromosomes occured normally, results in spermatids containing 2 or 4 haploid nuclei paired with a single abnormally large nebenkern ( Fig. 1B–C ). Defects in cytokinesis, coupled with abnormal chromosome segregation, give rise to spermatids containing a large nebenkern associated with multiple nuclei of variable size ( Figure. 1D ). In this context, one peculiarity of male meiotic cells that is particularly advantageous when studying cytokinesis, is the lack of a robust spindle assembly checkpoint ( Rebollo and Gonzalez, 2000 ). The spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) signaling pathway normally monitors kinetochore attachment to spindle microtubules ( Musacchio and Salmon, 2007 ). In somatic cells, SAC blocks cells in metaphase in the presence of unattached chromosomes or malformed spindles. In spermatocytes, in contrast, such abnormalities only cause a small delay in anaphase onset. Thus, the spermatocyte system allows discovery of requirements for gene products in later stages of cell division that are masked in somatic cells by the strong SAC when those same gene products also have an earlier role in spindle assembly or bipolar attachment of kinetochores to spindle microtubules (Wakefield et al., 2000; Giansanti et al., 2001 ).


## Cleavage furrow formation in Drosophila spermatocytes
The pioneering studies of Ray Rappaport (1961) indicated for the first time the key role of anaphase spindle microtubules in establishing the cleavage site in animal cells. Anaphase mitotic spindles are largely comprised of astral microtubules, which emanate radially from the two separated centrosomes toward the poles of the cell, and the central spindle (CS), an array of antiparallel, interdigitating microtubule (MT) bundles that lies between the two sets of chromosomes ( Glotzer, 2009 ). The cleavage furrow forms in a position that bisects the axis of chromosome segregation, thus ensuring that each daughter cell receives a single copy of the genome ( Glotzer; 2009 ). While CS microtubules send stimulatory signals to the cell equator for contractile ring assembly, astral microtubules are thought to send inhibitory signal(s) that prevent the localization of contractile ring proteins at the poles of the cell ( Dechant and Glotzer, 2003 ; Bringmann and Hyman, 2005 ; Lewellyn et al., 2010 ). Cytological analysis of Drosophila mutants defective in early steps of spermatocyte cytokinesis has revealed the importance of central spindle microtubules in cleavage furrow formation ( Giansanti et al, 1999 ; Giansanti et al., 2004 ). In spermatocytes, central spindle microtubules are the only spindle components that are strictly required for signaling cytokinesis. Dividing spermatocytes devoid of astral microtubules and anucleate secondary spermatocytes assemble regular central spindles and contractile rings and successfully complete cytokinesis ( Bonaccorsi et al., 1998 ; Giansanti et al., 2008 ; Bucciarelli et al., 2003 ). In contrast, mutations that perturb central spindle assembly invariably result in defects in F-actin ring assembly and cause early cytokinesis failure ( Giansanti et al, 1999 ; Giansanti et al., 2004 ). As in other animal cells, major central spindle components include MAPs, plus-end directed kinesins, and the chromosomal passenger complex in addition to the antiparallel microtubule array. Among the MAPs, the Drosophila ortholog of the highly conserved P rotein R egulating C ytokinesis I (PRC1), Fascetto (Feo), is one of the first markers that localizes to the overlap central region of the anaphase central spindle in spermatocytes ( Vernì et al, 2004 ; Szafer-Glusman et al., 2011 ). Although not studied in spermatocytes, Feo is necessary for central spindle assembly in both larval neuroblasts and S2 cells ( Vernì et al, 2004 ). Two other MAPs were found to contribute to central spindle stability in Drosophila spermatocytes, namely Orbit and A bnormal S pindle P oles (Asp). Orbit localizes to the central spindle midzone but, in contrast to Feo, it associates with a subset of microtubule bundles that reside inside the nuclear envelope, dubbed “interior central spindle” microtubules ( Inoue et al., 2004 ). orbit mutant spermatocytes display defective interior central spindle bundles and fail to complete contractile ring constriction. The evolutionarily conserved MAP, Asp (known as ASPM in humans), is concentrated at the polar regions of spindles in both neuroblasts and spermatocytes and contributes to spindle pole formation ( Saunders et al., 1997 ; Wakefield et al., 2001 ). During anaphase, this protein localizes at the ends of the central spindle microtubule array nearest to the separated chromosomes, where it might crosslink and stabilize central spindle microtubule minus ends ( Wakefield et al., 2001 ; Riparbelli et al., 2002 ). Indeed, loss of asp function perturbs central spindle structure and impairs contractile ring assembly in spermatocytes ( Wakefield et al., 2001 ) Several plus-end directed microtubule motor proteins of the kinesin family are also structural components of the spermatocyte central spindle. Both KLP3A (the Drosophila ortholog of KIF4) and the kinesin 6 family member MKLP1/Pavarotti (Pav) localize at the center of the central spindle, where the microtubule plus-ends overlap in anaphase spermatocytes ( Williams et al., 1995 ; for a review see D’Avino et al., 2005 ). KLP3A, like its homolog KIF4, forms a conserved complex with the MAP Feo/PRC1 and is required for formation of both the central spindle and the contractile ring ( D’Avino et al., 2007 ; Giansanti et al., 1998 ; Giansanti et al., 1999 ). A key regulatory component for specifying the site of cleavage furrow formation is centralspindlin, composed of two molecules of the kinesin family member Pav and two molecules of the Rho family GAP RacGAP50C. Centralspindlin activates RhoA (named Rho1 in Drosophila ) at the equatorial cortex to trigger actomyosin contractile ring assembly. During cytokinesis, the balance between the active state (GTP-bound) and inactive state (GDP-bound) of RhoA/Rho1 depends on the RhoGEF ECT2/Pebble, which is targeted to the equatorial cortex by binding to centralspindlin ( Somers and Saint, 2003 ; Kamijo et al., 2006 ; Nishimura and Yonemura, 2006 ; Yuce et al., 2005; Zhao and Fang, 2005; Piekny et al., 2005 ). Regulation of centralspindlin activity and recruitment of ECT2/Pebble involve the activities of Aurora B kinase and Polo-like kinase 1 (Plk1). During anaphase, Aurora B-mediated phosphorylation of MKLP1/Pavarotti is critical for centralspindlin formation ( Guse et al., 2005 ; Neef et al., 2006 ; Douglas et al., 2010 ), while targeting of the RhoGEF ECT2/Pebble to the cortex depends, at least in part, on phosphorylation of MgRacGAP/RacGAP50 by Polo kinase ( Brennan et al., 2007 ; Santamaria et al, 2007 ; Burkard et al., 2009 ; Wolfe et al., 2009 ). Plk1 and Aurora B also control early events of cell division such as chromosome condensation, spindle formation and chromosome segregation (for reviews see Barr et al., 2004 ; van der Waal et al., 2012 ). Because of these earlier requirements, the analysis of the effects of mutants lacking either Polo or Aurora B on cytokinesis in most cell types has been hindered by the early mitotic arrest caused by activation of the spindle checkpoint (Llamazares et al., 1980; Giet and Glover, 2001 ). In Drosophila spermatocytes, however, the lack of stringent checkpoint control has enabled investigation of the functional roles of the Polo and Aurora B kinases during cytokinesis. Drosophila mutants carrying a hypomorphic polo allele are viable and progress through male meiosis, thus allowing investigation of the function of Polo in cytokinesis ( Carmena et al., 1998 ). During anaphase, Polo accumulates at the spindle midzone and is required for proper localization of Pav and for formation of both the central spindle and F-actin ring ( Carmena et a., 1998 ; Hermann et al., 1998). Although there are no extant viable pav alleles that could be examined in male meiosis, the Pav and Polo proteins interact structurally, and each depends on the other for localization to the central spindle midzone in Drosophila embryonic cells ( Adams et al., 1998 ). Aurora B is the enzymatic subunit of the Chromosomal passenger Complex (CPC). Three additional CPC subunits, Inner Centromere Protein, Survivin, and Borealin, localize and activate the kinase ( Ruchaud et al., 2007a ; van der Waal et al., 2012 ). Early in mitosis and meiosis, in the events leading to metaphase, the CPC regulates processes such as chromosome condensation, spindle formation, correct attachment of kinetochore-to-spindle microtubules, and the mitotic checkpoint ( Ruchaud et al., 2007a ). During anaphase, function of the CPC is required for central spindle assembly and cytokinesis ( Carmena, 2008 ). Consistent with its multiple roles during cell division, the CPC exhibits very dynamic, cell cycle-dependent localization patterns. The CPC associates with chromatin during G2, then moves to the inner centromeres by prometaphase-metaphase. In anaphase the CPC translocates to the central spindle microtubules and the equatorial cortex ( Szafer-Glusman et al., 2011 ; Adams et al., 2000 ; Vagnarelli and Earnshaw, 2004 ; Vader et al., 2006 ; van der Waal et al., 2012 ). The D. melanogaster genome encodes two Borealin paralogs, Borealin-related (Borr) and Australin (Aust; Gao et al., 2008 ); the latter replaces Borr in male meiosis. Dividing spermatocytes from males carrying either a hypomorphic allele of dmINCENP or a null allele of australin (aust) display a similar phenotype, early cytokinesis arrest with defective central spindles and failure to recruit Pav to the cell equator ( Resnick et al., 2006 ; Gao et al., 2008 ), thereby suggesting a role for the CPC in activation and/or localization of the centralspindlin complex. The strict interdependence of the four CPC subunits ( Ruchaud et al., 2007a ; 2007b ) has complicated the analysis of the function of each single component; indeed, in human cells, knockdown of each CPC protein impaired the localization and function of the whole complex ( Adams et al., 2001 ; Carvalho et al., 2003 ; Honda et al.; 2003 ; Lens et al; 2003; Gassmann et al., 2004 ). A “separation-of-function” allele of Drosophila Survivin/Deterin ( dSurvivin ), isolated after EMS mutagenesis, has allowed investigation of the role of Survivin, and by inference the CPC, specifically during anaphase and cytokinesis ( Szafer-Glusman et al., 2011 ). The scapolo (scpo) mutation contains a missense mutation in the BIR domain of the Survivin polypetide that allows recruitment and function of the CPC in metaphase, but disrupts its localization and function in anaphase and cytokinesis ( Szafer-Glusman et al., 2011 ). Interestingly, phenotypic analysis of scapolo (scpo) hemizygotes revealed striking differences in how determination of the site of cleavage furrow formation is regulated in different Drosophila cell types ( Fig. 2 ). In both neuroblasts and spermatocytes at anaphase, Survivin is required to target the CPC and Pav to the central spindle and the equatorial cortex. In anaphase spermatocytes, the scpo mutation also impaired localization of Polo and Rho1 to the cell equatorial cortex and contractile ring assembly. These results suggest that in male meiotic cells Survivin is involved in the mechanism that leads to Rho1 activation. This may be through effects on Polo, as failure to recruit Polo would prevent localization of the RhoGEF ECT2/Pebble, which normally activates Rho1 locally, thereby resulting in the inability to form a contractile ring. In contrast, in anaphase neuroblasts from scpo hemizygotes, Polo, Rho1, and Myosin II accumulate in a broad equatorial cortical band, even in the absence of CPC and Pav. Such an initial wide equatorial belt of cytokinetic machinery components is also formed in wild type neuroblasts undergoing mitosis ( Fig. 2 ). This initial wide band normally narrows gradually into a thin ring at the equatorial cell cortex, then the ring constricts for cleavage furrow formation during cytokinesis. In scpo neuroblasts, however, the broad equatorial band, visualized by Myosin II, failed to convert into the thin equatorial contractile ring and the mutant cells did not complete cytokinesis. These observations indicate a striking, cell type-specific difference in the events that set up the contractile ring. In neuroblasts, assembly of the contractile ring occurs in two steps, as proposed by Lewellyn et al. (2011) for C. elegans embryos. The first step, which involves activation and localization of Rho1 and the recruitment of contractile ring proteins to a broad swath of the cortex about the midriff of the cell, appears independent of the central spindle and Pav ( Fig. 2 ). Spermatocytes, in contrast, appear to skip the first step: Rho1 and contractile ring proteins first appear at the equatorial cortex in a tightly focused ring, without forming a preceding wide band ( Fig. 2A ). It may be that global inhibition of Rho activation is more stringent at the cortex of spermatocytes than in neuroblasts, where a more permissive cortex could allow early formation of a broad belt of Rho1 ( Fig. 2B ). Alternatively, a recent study has shown the existence in neuroblasts of a spindle-independent backup system that permits localization of Myosin II to the cell cortex under the control of the neuroblast cell polarity system ( Cabernard et al., 2010 ). This redundant signaling system, not present in spermatocytes, may account for the broad band of Myosin II observed in scpo mutant neuroblasts during anaphase.


## Structural and regulatory components of the contractile ring in Drosophila spermatocytes
In the GTP bound state, Rho activates downstream effectors that lead to actin polymerization and Myosin II activation at the equatorial cortex ( Piekny et al., 2005 ; Goode and Eck, 2007 ; Matsumara et al., 2011 ). Non-muscle Myosin II in the contractile ring consists of hexamers that assemble into filaments. Each hexamer contains a dimer of heavy chains, two essential light chains and two regulatory light chains. Phosphorylation of the regulatory light chains (rMLC) is a key step required for activation of Myosin II motor activity and bipolar filament assembly. The importance of this process is demonstrated by the opposite effects of phosphomimetic and nonphosphorylatable alleles of the Drosophila rMLC Spaghetti Squash (Sqh) on cytokinesis ( Jordan and Karess, 1997 ). Dividing spermatocytes from mutant males in which function of sqh has been lost are devoid of both central spindles and F-actin rings at telophase, indicating an early requirement for Sqh in cytokinesis ( Giansanti et al., 2001 ). The traditional “purse-string” model for how the contractile ring generates the force for cleavage furrow ingression proposes that bipolar filaments of Myosin II use their motor activity to walk along antiparallel actin filaments, drawing the F-actin strands together in a purse-string-like fashion ( Schroeder et al., 1990 ; Satterwhite and Pollard, 1992 ). Because the cortex/plasma membrane is attached to the F-actin strands, the membrane is drawn inward into a cleavage furrow as the actomyosin ring constricts just under the cell cortex. However, the exact geometry of F-actin and Myosin II filaments in the contractile ring is still a matter of debate and alternative models for contractile ring organization and constriction have been proposed ( Reichl et al., 2008 ; Carvalho et al., 2009 ; Kee et al. 2012 ; Ma et al., 2012 ; reviewed in Green et al., 2012). Recent studies in Dictyostelium and vertebrate cells have led to the proposal that Myosin II does not translocate actin, but is required to cross-link actin filaments and to exert tension on actin during cytokinesis ( Reichl et al., 2008 ; Kee et al., 2012 ; Ma et al., 2012 ). As the ring constricts, the cross-sectional area of the ring is stable, suggesting the importance of a balance between F-actin polymerization and disassembly during cytokinesis. F-actin ring assembly and disassembly are regulated by the actin-binding proteins Profilin and Cofilin. Profilin binds to actin monomers and promotes actin filament formation in a formin-dependent pathway at the cell cortex (reviewed in Glotzer; 2005). Cofilin is an actin-severing protein required for F-actin dissassembly (Ono et al., 2003). Consistent with the functions of these proteins, mutations affecting profilin and cofilin have opposite effects on cytokinesis in spermatocytes. Mutations in the gene chickadee (chic) , which encodes the Drosophila profilin, impair F-actin ring formation and central spindle assembly ( Giansanti et al., 1998 ; Giansanti et al., 1999 ). Mutations in the gene twinstar ( tsr ), which encodes a cofilin homolog, do not affect contractile ring assembly, but perturb F-actin disassembly at the end of cytokinesis ( Gunsalus et al., 1995 ; Giansanti et al., 1999 ). Telophase spermatocytes from tsr males display abnormally large F-actin aggregates that fail to disassemble at the end of cytokinesis ( Gunsalus et al., 1995 ). In addition to Myosin II and F-actin, other structural components of the contractile apparatus, including the GTP-binding Septins and Anillin, act as scaffolding proteins at the cleavage furrow (for a review see D’Avino, 2009 ). Five D. melanogaster septins have been identified so far: Sep1, Sep2, Peanut, Sep4 and Sep5 ( Adam et al., 2000 ). In testes, Peanut, Sep1 and Sep2 colocalize with Myosin II, Anillin and F-actin in the contractile rings, and are also components of the ring canals that remain to connect sister cells after the incomplete cytokinesis typical of mitotic spermatogonia and the meiotic divisions of spermatocytes ( Hime et al., 1996 ; Giansanti et al., 2001 ). However, the role played by Septins in Drosophila spermatocytes has not been fully elucidated ( Adam et al., 2000 ; Giansanti et al., 2001 ). Anillin was first isolated as an F-actin-binding protein from Drosophila embryo extracts ( Miller et al., 1989 ). Later studies showed that this widely-conserved protein also binds activated Myosin II and Septins, which are also filament-forming proteins ( Kinoshita et al., 2002 ; Straight et al., 2005 ; D’Avino et al., 2008 ; reviewed in D’Avino et al., 2009). Recent studies have provided evidence suggesting that Drosophila Anillin interacts with RacGAP50C ( D’Avino et al., 2008 ; Gregory et al., 2008 ). Based on these data, it has been suggested that Anillin might mediate interactions between central spindle microtubules and the equatorial cortex required to stabilize the division plane. In Drosophila spermatocytes, Anillin starts to concentrate at the cell equator of dividing spermatocytes during anaphase, before the appearance of F-actin ( Giansanti et al., 1999 ; Fig. 1 ). Consistent with this temporal order, analysis of spermatocytes from mutants defective in F-actin ring assembly showed that initial recruitment of Anillin to the cortex did not depend on F-actin ( Giansanti et al., 1999 ). Analysis of dividing spermatocytes depleted of Anillin by Goldbach and coauthors (2010) showed that Anillin function is required for localization of Septins to the cleavage furrow and to maintain Rho, F-actin and Myosin II in the contractile ring. Strikingly, ectopic expression of the adherens junction protein DE-cadherin suppressed the cytokinesis defects caused by loss of anillin in spermatocytes ( Goldbach et al., 2010 ). DE-cadherin-GFP localized to the cleavage furrow and recruited β-catenin and α-catenin during cytokinesis, raising the possibility that either anillin-septin or cadherin-catenin complexes act as alternative cassettes to anchor F-actin and Myosin II at the cleavage furrow during spermatocyte cytokinesis. A novel component of Drosophila contractile ring and ring canals, dubbed Nessun Dorma (Nesd), may play a role in anchoring the actomyosin ring to the plasma membrane during cleavage furrow ingression ( Montembault et al., 2010 ). Nesd, identified as an evolutionarily-conserved partner of the centralspindlin complex proteins ( Montembault et al., 2010 ), directly binds both Pav and RacGAP50C through a highly-conserved domain in its N-terminus. Phenotypic analysis of dividing spermatocytes from nesd mutants revealed that nesd function is required during late telophase in male meiotic cells. Nesd starts to concentrate in the cleavage furrow during late telophase and is required for ring canal formation ( Montembault et al., 2010 ). Nesd function in ring canals might be associated with the presence of a pectin lyase-like domain in its C-terminal half and its high binding affinity for β̃-galactosides. Based on these results, it has been suggested that glycosylated proteins are involved in late cytokinesis of Drosophila male germ cells ( Montembault et al., 2010 ). Consistent with this hypothesis, the glycoprotein mucin D is a component of the ring canals in both testes and ovaries of Drosophila ( Kramerova and Kramerov, 1999 ).


## Membrane traffic in male meiotic cytokinesis
In addition to the force generated by constriction of the actomyosin contractile ring at the cell equator, successful cleavage furrow ingression requires an increase in surface area of the plasma membrane to encapsulate each daughter cell into a separate membrane compartment. Indeed, recent studies have demonstrated that targeted vesicle transport plays an important role during cleavage furrow ingression, as well as in the later events of abscission ( McKay and Burgess, 2011 ; Neto et al., 2011 ). Vesicle-mediated traffic to the ingressing cleavage furrow involves components of two main pathways: the recycling endosome (RE) pathway and the secretory pathway (reviewed in Prekeris and Gould, 2008 ; McKay and Burgess, 2011 ). A large part of our knowledge regarding the role of vesicle trafficking during cytokinesis has derived from experiments performed on cell lines grown in culture. Drosophila male meiosis offers a well-suited model for studying the requirement of membrane trafficking during cytokinesis in a tissue of a multicellular organism. Spermatocyte cytokinesis is especially sensitive to mutations affecting vesicle trafficking components, as reflected by the large number of male sterile mutants defective in male meiotic cytokinesis that have been identified in mutant screens in Drosophila ( Brill et al., 2000 ; Xu et al., 2002 ; Farkas et al., 2003 ; Gatt and Glover; 2006 ; Giansanti et al., 2006 , 2007 ; Dyer et al, 2007 ; Polevoy et al., 2009 ; Robinett et al., 2009 ). Secretory traffic, involving vesicle transport from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) to the Golgi, and then to the plasma membrane, plays a crucial role in spermatocyte cytokinesis ( Xu et al., 2002 ; Farkas et al., 2003 ; Giansanti et al., 2006 , 2007 ; Belloni et al, 2012). Based on the transmission electron microscopy studies of Tates (1971) , the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) of almost mature spermatocytes, just prior to the meiotic divisions, is arranged in a system of parallel cisternae around the nucleus, with one of the cisternae continuous with the nuclear envelope. During meiotic division, ER structure can be visualized in spermatocytes from males that express the ER marker Protein disulfide isomerase (Pdi) tagged with GFP ( Bobinnec et al., 2003 ; Giansanti et al., 2006 , 2007 ). In metaphase and ana-telophase, GFP-Pdi stains the parafusorial membranes that surround the nuclear envelope (see above) and a system of 13 umbrella-shaped layers at each cell pole dubbed the “astral membranes” (AM) ( Giansanti et al., 2006 , 2007 ). Golgi complexes in premeiotic primary spermatocytes are associated with the ER cisternae ( Tates, 1971 ). One peculiarity of Drosophila cells (including spermatocytes) is the organization of Golgi in multiple separate stacks ( Tates, 1971 ; Fuller, 1993 ; Kondylis and Rabouille, 2009 ). These approximately 20 small Golgi stacks can be visualized as conical structures with antibodies against the Golgi scaffolding protein Lava lamp (Lva) or the Golgi mannosidase α-mannosidase II (GMII) (Sisson et al., 2002; Farkas et al., 2003 ; Giansanti et al., 2006 , 2007 ; Robinett et al., 2009 ). This characteristic organization in multiple, separate stacks is advantageous in phenotypic analysis of mutants, as it allows the detection of subtle structural defects that might be missed in mammalian cells with Golgi stacks interconnected into a pericentriolar Golgi ribbon ( Kondylis and Rabouille, 2009 ). By metaphase to telophase, the Golgi are dispersed and Golgi markers are associated with small vesicle-like structures that are excluded from the cell midzone ( Farkas et al., 2003 ; Giansanti et al., 2006 , 2007 ). Several studies have implicated Golgi trafficking in cytokinesis in male meiotic cells. For example, treatment of cultured testes with Brefeldin A, a fungal metabolite that interferes with anterograde transport from the ER to the Golgi, causes disruption of Golgi organelles and disrupts cytokinesis during meiosis I and II, as revealed by the presence of multinucleate spermatids ( Robinett et al., 2009 ; Kitazawa et al., 2012 ). In addition, cytokinesis of spermatocytes requires the wild type function of several proteins implicated in Golgi trafficking. These include the intra-Golgi trafficking Cog5 and Cog7 homologs ( Farkas et al., 2003 , Belloni et al., 2012), the ER-to-Golgi vesicle docking protein Syntaxin 5 ( Xu et al., 2002 ), and Brunelleschi, which encodes the Drosophila ortholog of the yeast TRAPP II TRS120p subunit ( Robinett et al., 2009 ). Recent studies have also implicated proteins that function in retrograde transport from Golgi to ER in spermatocyte cytokinesis, including subunits of the coatomer protein complex I (COPI) and the small GTPase Arf1 ( Kitazawa et al., 2012 ). Using markers for cis-Golgi and ER compartments, Kitazawa and coauthors found that COPI proteins localize in the ER-Golgi intermediate compartments of premeiotic spermatocytes. During metaphase, COPI proteins are associated with vesicles that follow a dynein-mediated poleward movement along astral microtubules. Testis-specific knockdown of COPI disrupted the localization of other membrane trafficking proteins and lipid droplets at the furrow. A striking finding of this study is the intimate relationship between the ER-based intracellular structures and spindle microtubules in dividing spermatocytes. Time lapse analysis of spermatocytes that express GFP-tagged tubulin and ER markers revealed that knockdown of COPI affects not only the structure of ER-based astral and parafusorial membranes, but also that of astral and central spindle microtubules. Conversely, in a tubulin mutant, a primary defect in microtubule structures resulted in secondary defects in ER-based structures. Based on these results, Kitazawa and coauthors (2012) suggested that ER membranes and microtubule bundles are mutually dependent structures during male meiotic division. Drosophila Zw10, a subunit of the conserved kinetochore-associated Rod-Zwilch-Zw10 (RZZ) complex required for function of the spindle assembly checkpoint in both Drosophila and mammals ( Starr et al., 1998 ; Scaerou et al., 2001 ; Williams et al., 2003 ; reviewed in Karess, 2005 ; Musacchio and Salmon, 2007 ), has also recently been shown to play a role in membrane trafficking during cytokinesis in spermatocytes ( Wainman et al., 2012 ). In both mammalian and yeast cells, the Zw10 orthologs form a second complex with the conserved RINT1/Tip20p and NAG/Sec39p proteins that is involved in vesicle transport between Golgi and ER ( Hirose et al., 2004 ; Arasaki et al., 2006 ; Sun et al., 2007 ; Varma et al., 2006 ; Aoki et al., 2009 ; Schmitt, 2010 ). In Drosophila spermatocytes, Zw10 is enriched at both the Golgi stacks and the ER, and function of Zw10 and the Drosophila homologue of RINT1 are both required for Golgi integrity. Function of both Zw10 and RINT1 is essential for furrow ingression during cytokinesis in spermatocytes ( Wainman et al., 2012 ). In contrast, Rod is required for proper Golgi function, but is dispensable for cytokinesis. These data suggest that Zw10 and Rint1 might associate to form a subcomplex involved in membrane trafficking during spermatocyte cytokinesis that either does not contain or does not require active Rod. The small GTPases Rab11 and Arf6 are also essential for cytokinesis in Drosophila male meiotic cells ( Giansanti et al., 2007 ). In spermatocytes, Rab11 has been implicated in both Golgi and recycling endocytic traffic, and is enriched at vesicles that mediate membrane addition at the cleavage furrow ( Giansanti et al., 2007 ; Dyer et al., 2007 ; Polevoy, 2009 ). In dividing spermatocytes, ARF6 colocalized with Rab11 and with the early endosome marker Rab4 ( Dyer et al., 2007 ). Loss of ARF6 did not affect localization of Rab4 and Rab11 to the spindle midzone, but did disrupt completion of cleavage furrow ingression. Remarkably, ARF6 directly binds Pav, raising the possibility that centralspindlin might help recruit ARF6-associated endosomes to the cleavage furrow ( Dyer et al., 2007 ). Cytological analysis of mutants in fws, bru, Rab11, Zw10 and Arf6 revealed a common cytokinetic phenotype: mutant spermatocytes assemble regular central spindles and contractile rings during early telophase, but the rings fail to complete constriction, thereby resulting in cytokinesis failure ( Farkas et al., 2003 ; Giansanti et al., 2007 ; Dyer et al., 2007 ; Robinett et al., 2009 ; Wainman et al., 2012 ). In addition, time-lapse analysis of arf6 , rab11 and zw10 mutant spermatocytes expressing β-tubulin-EGFP indicated similar defects in central spindles over time ( Dyer et al., 2007 ; Giansanti et al., 2007 ; Wainman et al., 2012 ). Central spindles formed transiently and furrows initially ingressed during the early stages of cytokinesis in the mutants. However, later during telophase, cleavage furrows regressed, and central spindle microtubule bundles disassembled. This failure to complete cytokinesis and the subsequent furrow regression phenotype are thought to arise from defects in membrane addition to the surface at the furrow. In wild type spermatocytes, the increase in perimeter during cytokinesis correlates well with surface area increase ( Dyer et al., 2007 ). The perimeter increase of arf6 and zw10 mutant spermatocytes during cytokinesis was drastically affected, consistent with defects in membrane expansion in the mutant cells ( Dyer et al., 2007 ; Wainman et al., 2012 ). The large size of spermatocytes and the requirement that these cells must undergo two successive meiotic divisions in a restricted time window with only a very short intervening interphase ( Fuller, 1993 ; Giansanti et al., 2001 ) may have contributed to the emergence of Drosophila spermatocytes as a very attractive system for investigating the roles of membrane remodeling and vesicle trafficking in cytokinesis. In addition, because spermatocytes lack plasma membrane protrusions and microvilli ( Tates, 1971 ) that might be used as a plasma membrane reservoir during cytokinesis, they may rely more on traffic from internal membrane stores to provide the additional surface area to produce four cells from one. Whatever the underlying reason, multiple mutations identified in forward genetic screens have revealed that the meiotic divisions of Drosophila spermatocytes appear to be particularly sensitive to mutations affecting vesicle trafficking pathways ( Brill et al., 2000 ; Xu et al., 2002 ; Farkas et al., 2003 ; Gatt and Glover; 2006 ; Giansanti et al., 2006 ,; Giansanti et al., 2007 ; Dyer et al, 2007 ; Robinett et al., 2009 ; Polevoy et al., 2009 ).


## Specialized lipids and cytokinesis
Successful cytokinesis also depends on a special lipid composition at the cleavage furrow. Special membrane domains are emerging as key factors in regulating both the dynamics of cytokinetic structures and membrane addition (reviewed in Neto et al. 2011 ). Very-long-chain-fatty acids (VLCFAs), consisting of 22 or more carbons, are primarily found in sphingolipids, and participate in the stabilization of highly curved membrane domains such as those at the ingressing tip of the cleavage furrow ( Schneiter et al., 2004 ). The Drosophila gene bond (named after James Bond because of the lack of a ring) encodes a member of the Elovl family involved in the biosynthesis of VLCFAs ( Szafer-Glusman et al., 2008 ). In bond mutant spermatocytes, central spindles fail to assemble and the contractile ring, visualized by Sqh-GFP, detaches from the membrane and collapses to one side of the cell ( Szafer-Glusman et al., 2008 ). These findings suggest that VLCFAs are required to allow the plasma membrane to undergo cleavage furrow invagination and indicate an intimate relationship between membrane lipids and the dynamics of cytoskeletal structures during cytokinesis. In contrast with bond , mutants in peroxin genes ( pex ) accumulate elevated levels of VLCFAs and pex/+ heterozygotes suppress the cytokinesis defects in bond homozygotes ( Chen et al., 2010 ). However, dividing spermatocytes from males carrying pex mutations fail cytokinesis just like bond, indicating that cytokinesis requires proper regulation of VLCFA levels. Phosphatidylinositol phosphates are also important signaling molecules during cytokinesis (for a review see Brill et al., 2011 ). The Drosophila phosphatidylinositol (PI) transfer protein Giotto/Vibrator (Gio) concentrates in both the ER membranes and the cleavage furrow in dividing spermatocytes. Spermatocytes and neuroblasts from gio mutants are defective in contractile ring constriction and exhibit an accumulation of Golgi-derived vesicles at the cleavage site, suggesting a defect in membrane addition ( Gatt and Glover, 2006 ; Giansanti et al., 2006 ; Giansanti et al., 2007 ). The phosphoinositide Phosphatidylinositol 4,5-biphosphate (PIP2), enriched in the plasma membrane of the cleavage furrow of Drosophila spermatocytes, is crucial for cleavage furrow stability ( Wong et al., 2005 ). Compelling data indicate that PIP2 promotes actin polymerization (reviewed in Yin and Janmey, 2003 ). In addition, the polybasic domain of Septins and the pleckstrin homology domain of Anillin can mediate interactions with PIP2 ( Bertin et al., 2010 ; Liu et al., 2012 ). Thus, disruption of PIP2 at the furrow might affect the recruitment and/or the stability of contractile ring proteins. PIP2 turnover is also important during cytokinesis; drugs that interfere with PIP2 hydrolysis mediated by phospholipase C affect the completion of furrow ingression in spermatocytes ( Wong et al., 2005 ). Genetic analysis in Drosophila also indicates that phosphatidylinositol 4-phosphate (PI4P) may play a key role in cytokinesis of male meiotic cells. fwd encodes a Drosophila PI4KIIIβ (Brill et al., 2002). Dividing spermatocytes from fwd mutant males exhibit a cytokinetic phenotype similar to that of gio and Rab11 mutants ( Giansanti et al., 2006 ; Giansanti et al., 2007 ; Polevoy et al., 2009 ). Phenotypic analysis of single and double mutants in fwd, rab11, and gio indicate that Rab11, Gio and Fwd might function in the same pathway during cytokinesis, with Gio and Fwd required upstream of Rab11 ( Giansanti et al., 2007 ). Indeed, Fwd proteins bind the GTPase Rab11 and colocalize with both Rab11 and PI4P markers on Golgi membranes, and loss of fwd disrupts the localization of Rab11 and PI4P at the cell equator ( Polevoy et al., 2009 ).


## Conclusions and perspectives
Drosophila spermatocytes have proven to be an optimal cell system for dissecting the molecular mechanisms underlying cytokinesis in the context of a multicellular organism. Studies over the last two decades have revealed the complexity of this process, which requires tight coordination of cell cycle regulatory proteins, cytoskeletal components and membrane trafficking. Genetic dissection of male meiotic cytokinesis in Drosophila has allowed identification of the key molecular players. However, we are still far from identifying all the pieces of this complex puzzle. Male meiotic divisions are particularly dependent on membrane trafficking from internal membrane compartments, making this an excellent model for investigating membrane remodeling during cytokinesis, and pursuing the open questions concerning membrane addition to the cleavage site (reviewed in Mckay and Burgess, 2012) Remarkably, several cytokinesis genes/proteins have also been found to play roles in cancer pathogenesis (reviewed in Normand and King, 2010 ; Lacroix and Maddox, 2012 ). Cytokinesis proteins are frequently upregulated in cancer cells or are encoded by genes that are deleted or amplified in human cancers ( Roversi et al., 2006 ; Lind et al., 2011 ; Sinha et al., 2011 ). Thus, clarification of the molecular pathways involved in cytokinesis may also aid cancer diagnosis and the design of therapeutic strategies.
