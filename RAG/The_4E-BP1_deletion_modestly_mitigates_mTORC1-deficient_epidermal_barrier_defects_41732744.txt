TITLE: The 4E-BP1 deletion modestly mitigates mTORC1-deficient epidermal barrier defects
PMID: 41732744
PMC: 
------------------------------------------------------------

# ABSTRACT
mTOR signaling is essential for epidermal development, yet the downstream mechanisms mediating its effects remain unclear. The 4E-BP1 (eukaryotic initiation factor 4E-binding protein 1), a major mTORC1 target that inhibits cap-dependent translation, has an undefined role in epidermal morphogenesis. In this study, we investigated the impact of 4E-BP1 loss on mTORC1 inactivation–mediated skin barrier defects. Eif4ebp1 −/− mice were generated using CRISPR/Cas9-mediated genome editing and crossed with epidermal-specific Rptor knockout ( Rap EKO ) mice to obtain double-knockout (dKO) animals. Both Rap EKO and dKO neonates displayed fragile, shiny skin; rapid weight loss; and neonatal lethality. Toluidine blue assays revealed a partial restoration of barrier function in dKO mice. Histological analysis showed severe epidermal hypoplasia and loss of stratification in both mutants, whereas dKO mice exhibited mild and irregular cornification with slightly increased epidermal thickness. Proliferation remained impaired, but differentiation markers were partially restored in dKO epidermis. These findings demonstrate that 4E-BP1 deletion modestly alleviates the differentiation and barrier defects caused by mTORC1 inactivation but does not prevent neonatal lethality, suggesting that 4E-BP1 is not the major downstream effector of mTORC1 during epidermal morphogenesis.

# BODY
## Introduction
The epidermis constitutes the outermost layer of the skin and provides an essential barrier that protects the body from dehydration, mechanical injury, and microbial invasion ( Kubo et al, 2012 ; Watt, 2014 ). Rather than a static structure, this barrier is a dynamic, self-renewing interface maintained through the continuous proliferation, differentiation, and shedding of keratinocytes ( Hsu and Fuchs, 2022 ; Natsuga, 2014 ; Takaishi et al, 2025 ). At its foundation, basal keratinocytes anchored to the basement membrane function as progenitors, supplying differentiating cells that ascend to form the spinous, granular, and cornified layers ( Blanpain and Fuchs, 2009 ; Natsuga, 2014 ). The assembly and maintenance of this stratified architecture rely on finely tuned transcriptional and metabolic programs that integrate signals from the tissue microenvironment with systemic and mechanical cues ( Dai and Segre, 2004 ; Prado-Mantilla et al, 2025 ; Simpson et al, 2011 ). Disruption of these regulatory networks impairs barrier integrity, giving rise to developmental abnormalities and inflammatory skin disorders ( Che-Wen et al, 2025 ; Cibrian et al, 2020 ; Dai and Segre, 2004 ; Di Girolamo et al, 2025 ). Understanding how keratinocytes couple growth control to differentiation and barrier formation is therefore fundamental to deciphering the principles governing tissue homeostasis and regeneration. The mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin) signaling pathway, functioning through 2 distinct complexes: mTORC1 (mTOR complex 1) and mTORC2 (mTOR complex 2), serves as a central regulator of cellular growth, metabolism, and tissue homeostasis ( Battaglioni et al, 2022 ). Recent studies with both genetic studies in mice and pharmacological investigations in patients have underscored the pivotal role of mTOR signaling in epidermal morphogenesis and barrier formation ( Asrani et al, 2017 ; Ding et al, 2020 , 2016 ; Larcombe et al, 1990 ; Wang et al, 2022 ). In particular, mTORC1 functions as an indispensable regulator of early epidermal development. Epidermis-specific deletion of mTOR or Rptor , encoding Raptor, a core component of mTORC1, results in defective keratinocyte growth, loss of stratification, and a severely compromised barrier and the consequent neonatal lethality ( Asrani et al, 2017 ; Ding et al, 2016 ; Wang et al, 2022 ), highlighting that epidermal morphogenesis critically depends on the metabolic and biosynthetic outputs of mTORC1. Despite the critical role of mTORC1 signaling in skin biology, the downstream effectors that execute its function remain largely undefined. Among its best-characterized roles, mTORC1 regulates protein synthesis through inhibition of 4E-BP1 (eukaryotic translation initiation factor 4E-binding protein 1) ( Battaglioni et al, 2022 ). Phosphorylation of 4E-BP1 by mTORC1 releases eIF4E, thereby enabling cap-dependent translation, whereas unphosphorylated 4E-BP1 sequesters eIF4E and represses translation initiation ( Battaglioni et al, 2022 ). Notably, myocyte-specific 4E-BP1 deletion mitigates cardiac dysfunction resulting from mTORC1 inhibition ( Zhang et al, 2010 ). In head and neck squamous cell carcinomas, 4E-BP1 acts as a tumor suppressor, and 4E-BP1 dephosphorylation mediates the therapeutic response to mTOR inactivation ( Wang et al, 2019 ). These findings highlight the context-dependent functions of 4E-BP1 in coordinating growth control and stress adaptation. However, whether 4E-BP1 mediates the effects of mTORC1 in epidermal morphogenesis and barrier formation remains unknown. In this study, we investigated whether loss of 4E-BP1 could rescue the epidermal defects caused by mTORC1 inactivation. We generated body-wide 4E-BP1–knockout ( Eif4ebp1 −/− ) mice using CRISPR/Cas9-mediated genome editing and crossed them with K14-Cre–mediated epidermal-specific Rptor- knockout mice (Rap EKO ) to obtain double-knockout (dKO) mutants. Our results revealed that 4E-BP1 deletion modestly ameliorates structural and molecular defects caused by mTORC1 ablation but is insufficient to fully restore barrier function or prevent neonatal lethality. These results indicate that although 4E-BP1 contributes to the epidermal functions of mTORC1, additional downstream effectors are likely required to coordinate the metabolic and translational programs essential for proper epidermal differentiation and barrier homeostasis.


## Results and Discussion
To determine whether loss of 4E-BP1 could rescue the epidermal abnormalities resulting from mTORC1 deficiency, we established double-mutant mice combining epidermal mTORC1 inactivation with complete 4E-BP1 knockout. To achieve this, we first generated body-wide 4E-BP1–deficient mice ( Eif4ebp1 −/− ) using CRISPR/Cas9 technology. Eif4ebp1 −/− mice were born at Mendelian ratios and developed normally. The neonates were viable with grossly normal skin; further determination of the impact of 4E-BP1 loss on epidermal differentiation or barrier function will be addressed in future studies. As previously described, mice carrying a loxP-flanked allele encoding Rptor gene ( Rptor fl/fl ) were crossed with a transgenic mouse line expressing Cre recombinase under the control of the human keratin 14 (K14) promoter, resulting in epidermal-specific Rptor depletion, referred to as Rap EKO ( Figure 1 a) ( Dassule et al, 2000 ; Deng et al, 2021 ). We then crossed Rptor fl/fl , K14-Cr e, and Eif4ebp1 −/− mice to generate the double-mutants Rap EKO / Eif4ebp1 −/− (referred to as dKO) ( Figure 1 a). Gene recombination was confirmed by PCR analysis of genomic DNA extracted from the mouse tissues. Western blot analysis verified 4E-BP1 deficiency in mouse skin ( Figure 1 b). Consistent with earlier reports ( Ding et al, 2016 ; Wang et al, 2022 ), Rap EKO neonates displayed a fragile and shiny appearance and died within hours after birth. Unexpectedly, dKO neonates exhibited a similar phenotype ( Figure 1 c). Body weight measurements showed that both Rap EKO and dKO neonates underwent rapid weight loss compared with their littermate controls ( Figure 1 c). To assess epidermal barrier integrity, we applied the exogenous toluidine blue dye permeability assay. In newborn controls, the dye was excluded, consistent with intact epidermal barrier function. In contrast, dye penetration was readily observed in the skin of both Rap EKO and dKO neonates; however, dKO mice exhibited attenuated dye penetration, particularly on the dorsal surface ( Figure 1 d). These results indicated that both the Rap EKO and dKO mutants failed to establish a fully protective epidermal barrier, and the barrier integrity was partially rescued in dKO mutants. Histological analysis of skin sections from neonates further confirmed the macroscopic observations. Control newborns exhibited a fully stratified epidermis with distinct basal, spinous, granular, and cornified layers. In contrast, Rap EKO newborn skin contained only 1 or 2 cell layers with marked absence of stratification, significantly reduced epidermal thickness, and fewer hair follicles. Compared with Rap EKO , dKO epidermis showed increased cornification; however, the enhancement was heterogenous. Quantification analysis revealed slight increases in epidermal thickness and hair follicle numbers in dKO newborns. Yet, these changes did not reach statistical significance ( Figure 1 e). Similar histological patterns were observed in the tongue tissues ( Figure 1 e). To assess the activation status of mTORC1 signaling in the epidermis, we performed immune staining with antibodies against phosphorylated mTOR downstream effectors: ribosomal protein S6 and 4E-BP1. Immunohistochemical analysis revealed a marked reduction in phosphorylated S6 and phosphorylated 4E-BP1 signals in the mutant epidermis ( Figure 1 f). The hypoplastic epidermis in Rap EKO mice may partially result from a reduction in cell proliferation. Cell proliferation assessment using Ki-67 staining showed reduced proliferation in Rap EKO epidermis that was not restored by 4E-BP1 depletion ( Figure 2 a). We further performed immunostaining analysis of back skin sections from control, Rap EKO , and dKO mice to detect epidermal differentiation markers, including basal layer protein K14, the spinous layer cell protein keratin 10, and late granular cell proteins FLG and loricrin for terminal-differentiated cells. Compared with controls, Rap EKO mice showed single-layer K14 staining in the basal layer, with occasional keratin 10 positivity in a few suprabasal cells ( Figure 2 b). The protein levels of key markers for terminal differentiation, including FLG and loricrin, were markedly reduced in Rap EKO mice. The dKO mice exhibited, to some extent, restoration of K14, keratin 10, FLG, and loricrin expression compared with Rap EKO mice ( Figure 2 b), consistent with the partial restoration of the outside-in barrier function ( Figure 1 d). Although terminal differentiation genes were expressed, the epidermis of both mutants was unable to execute proper structural maturation and barrier formation. These findings indicate that mTORC1 primarily regulates the translational processes necessary for terminal differentiation to progress into a functional epidermal barrier, which are mediated in part through the downstream effector 4E-BP1. Our study demonstrates that although 4E-BP1 knockout modestly alleviates the epidermal defects caused by epidermal-specific mTORC1 inactivation, it fails to fully rescue the barrier function or prevent neonatal lethality. Unlike in myocytes, where 4E-BP1 deletion can rescue defects caused by Raptor deficiency ( Zhang et al, 2010 ), our findings suggest that 4E-BP1 is not the principal mediator of mTORC1 signaling in epidermal morphogenesis. In this study, we focused on 4E-BP1; however, the 4E-BP family also includes 4E-BP2 and 4E-BP3, which exhibit tissue-specific and context-dependent expression ( Pause et al, 1994 ). The potential contribution of these paralogs to translational control in the skin remains largely unexplored and may partly explain the incomplete rescue observed in the dKO mice. Future work employing advanced mechanistic approaches, such as ribosome profiling and quantitative translatomics, will be essential to delineate how cap-dependent translation regulates epidermal differentiation and barrier formation ( Gandin et al, 2022 ). In summary, our findings highlight the tissue-specific functions of mTORC1 signaling across different tissues. Future studies should explore alternative mTORC1 effectors and their interactions to better understand skin barrier formation and maintenance mechanisms.


## Materials and Methods
Animal To generate mice with epidermal-specific mTORC1 inactivation, mice homozygous for floxed Rptor alleles were crossed with a transgenic strain expressing Cre recombinase under the human K14 promoter ( Dassule et al, 2000 ). Control groups comprised littermates either lacking Cre expression or expressing Cre with heterozygous loxP-flanked Rptor alleles. Eif4ebp1 −/− mice were generated by CRISPR/Cas9-mediated whole-body homozygous 4E-BP1 knockout and were obtained from Shanghai Model Organisms. The guide RNA sequences used for Eif4ebp1 knockout were 5′-ACGCTCTTCAGCACCACCCCGGG-3′ and 5′-AATTCCACATTTCAGGGAGAGGG-3′. To generate double-mutant mice, we used a 2-step breeding strategy: first, crossing Rptor fl/fl or K14-Cre mice separately with Eif4ebp1 −/− mice to generate Rptor fl/fl / Eif4ebp1 −/− or K14-Cre/ Eif4ebp1 −/− offspring, and subsequently, Rptor fl/fl / Eif4ebp1 −/− mice were crossed with K14-Cre/ Eif4ebp1 −/− mice to generate K14-Cre/ Rptor fl/fl / Eif4ebp1 −/− (referred to as dKO). Genotyping was performed by PCR using specific primers on genomic DNA isolated from tail tips as previously described. The primers for genotyping are as follows: forward 5′- CTC AGT Fl GGT ATG TGC TCA G -3′ and reverse 5′- GGG TAC AGT ATG TCA GCA CAG -3′ for Raptor, forward 5′-TTC CTC AGG AGT GTC TTC GC -3′ and reverse 5′-GTC CAT GTC CTT CCT GAA GC -3′ for K14-Cre, forward 5′-CTGCCACCAAATCGGAGAGT-3′ and reverse 5′-GACCAGTAAACGTGCCAGGA-3′ for Eif4ebp1 (P7-P8), forward 5′-TTCTGCCACCGTCATCCCTA-3′ and reverse 5′-AGCTACCGAACCCCTCGAAT-3′ for Eif4ebp1 (F11-R12). All mice were bred and maintained under specific pathogen-free conditions. Body weight of newborns was monitored at room temperature. Dye permeability assays were performed with 1% toluidine blue dye as described previously ( Ding et al, 2016 ). All experiments were approved according to the Animal Experimentation Ethics Committee of Shanghai University. Immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence staining Mouse back skin samples were either fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde or embedded in Optimal Cutting Temperature compound (Tissue Tek) for subsequent sectioning (4 or 10 μm). Histological analysis was performed using standard H&E staining. For immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence, antigen retrieval was performed using citric acid buffer (pH 6.0). Tissue sections were permeabilized with 0.5% Triton X-100 in PBS for 10 minutes at room temperature, followed by blocking with 100% normal goat serum for 1 hour. Primary antibody incubation was conducted overnight at 4 °C. For immunofluorescence, secondary antibody detection was followed by nuclear counterstaining with DAPI (Solarbio). For immunohistochemistry, signals were visualized using 3,3′-diaminobenzidine with hematoxylin counterstaining. Negative controls omitted primary antibodies but underwent identical processing. All immunostaining analyses were performed on postnatal day 0 skin sections. Primary antibodies and dilutions included Ki-67 (1:200, Abcam, ab15580), K14 (1:500, Abcam, ab181595), loricrin (1:200, Invitrogen, PA5-30583), keratin 10 (1:200, Santa Cruz, SC-23877), FLG (1:250, BioLegend, #905804), phosphorylated S6 (1:200, Cell Signaling Technology, #5364s), and phosphorylated 4EBP1 (1:200, Cell Signaling Technology, #2855). Secondary antibodies and dilutions included horseradish peroxidase–conjugated goat antirabbit IgG (1:200, Guo Ding, IH-0001), horseradish peroxidase–conjugated goat antimouse IgG (1:200, Guo Ding, IH-0031), and Alexa Fluor 594–conjugated antirabbit IgG (1:1000, Invitrogen, A-11008). Nuclei were counterstained with hematoxylin or DAPI (1:1000, Thermo Fisher). Images were acquired using an Olympus IX71 inverted microscope. For quantitative analysis, 3–5 fields were evaluated per tissue section. Immunohistochemical staining intensity was quantified using ImageJ software and the IHC Toolbox plugin. For each sample, 3–5 regions of interest were manually selected within the epidermis at comparable anatomical regions. The mean gray value (inverse optical density) of each region of interest was measured after background subtraction. For each section, the values from all regions of interest were averaged to generate a single biological data point. Results are expressed as mean gray value (arbitrary units), where higher values reflect stronger staining intensity. Statistical analysis All data were represented as mean ± SEM. Statistical analysis was performed using 2-way ANOVA with Tukey’s correction for multiple comparisons. GraphPad Prism software was utilized for data analysis. Statistical significance was considered at ∗ P < .05.
