TITLE: Comparative transcriptome analysis of developmental stages and characterization of core RNAi-related genes in the spittlebug Mahanarva fimbriolata
PMID: 41491413
PMC: 
------------------------------------------------------------

# ABSTRACT
Mahanarva fimbriolata (Distant 1909) (Hemiptera: Cercopidae) is a major pest that reduces forage production in Brazil, yet few viable control alternatives exist. The RNA interference (RNAi) gene silencing method is a specific and efficient approach that may aid the development of a sustainable management strategy for this pest. It is known that some genes involved in the RNAi machinery are lacking in certain Hemiptera species; therefore, verifying their presence in each target species is necessary. Here, transcriptome assembly of Mahanarva fimbriolata at different developmental stages (egg, nymph and adult stages) was performed, differentially expressed genes were identified, RNAi-related genes described in the literature were annotated in the transcriptome, and coexpression network modeling for the identification of potential RNAi targets was performed. The analysis revealed that the most significant differences in gene expression were between samples in the egg stage and samples in the other development stages. Enriched Gene Ontology terms related to insect growth (e.g., cell division, metamorphosis and flight) and corresponding pathways (e.g., DNA replication and glycolysis/gluconeogenesis) were identified. Coexpression networks demonstrated the importance of biosynthetic hormone processes within specific modules and revealed potential silencing targets, including hub genes such as RPB7 and Talin-2. Transcript annotation and analysis revealed more than 20 genes related to five major RNAi-related processes and factors (dsRNA cleavage, endonucleases, dsRNA binding, dsRNA transport and uptake, and auxiliary RISC-associated or regulatory factors). This work provides a comprehensive molecular overview of metamorphosis in M. fimbriolata , confirms the presence of active RNAi machinery, and reveals potential targets for future gene silencing approaches. # BODY
## Background
The genus Mahanarva [ 1 ], belonging to the order Hemiptera, suborder Auchenorrhyncha, and family Cercopidae, comprises approximately 40 spittlebug species [ 2 ]. Several Mahanarva species are economically important because of their herbivory, notably Mahanarva fimbriolata , which impacts areas throughout almost all states of Brazil [ 3 ]. In Brazil, where pastures cover approximately 161 million hectares [ 4 ] and nearly 10 million hectares are devoted to sugarcane cultivation [ 5 ], M. fimbriolata causes severe damage to grass production in general, with annual losses reaching 70% in planted areas and costing an estimated US$ 2 billion, in addition to emerging as one of the major pests of sugarcane [ 6 ]. M. fimbriolata infestations typically begin in early spring and continue throughout the summer, as egg hatching depends on rainfall [ 7 ]. Eggs are laid in the soil and can enter diapause for several days to survive under adverse conditions such as cold temperatures or drought [ 8 ]. Like other hemipterans, M. fimbriolata undergoes incomplete metamorphosis (hemimetabolism) and transforms into nymphs before they reach the adult stage. This juvenile nymph stage comprises five instars, distinguished by increased head capsule size and the development of wing pads, ending with the nymph reaching the adult stage [ 9 ]. Spittlebugs are sap-sucking herbivores that extract nutrients from roots as nymphs and leaves as adults [ 10 ]. Saliva toxins injected during feeding disrupt photosynthesis, impacting host crop health at both life stages [ 11 ]. Nymphs also produce protective foam from their Malpighian tubules, deterring predators, preventing desiccation, and providing thermoregulation [ 12 ]. Host-plant resistance to Mahanarva spittlebugs is limited to a few native Brazilian grasses, notably those in the Paspalum genus [ 13 ]. This complex trait involves multiple genes, enzymes and pathways, as well as morphological characteristics [ 14 ], hindering selection and breeding efforts [ 15 ]. Currently employed control methods rely heavily on insecticides [ 16 ], whereas biological control, such as the use of Metarhizium anisopliae , remains inefficient because of the limited understanding of the physiological and ecological differences among M. fimbriolata and related spittlebugs [ 17 ], as well as the fungal sensitivity to temperature and solar radiation [ 18 ]. RNA interference (RNAi) technology offers a more targeted and sustainable alternative for widespread use [ 19 ]. RNAi is a posttranscriptional gene silencing mechanism that is initiated by the cleavage of double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) into small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) in the cytosol [ 20 ]. These siRNAs bind to the RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC) and target and degrade specific mRNA sequences, leading to gene repression [ 21 ]. While conserved across most eukaryotes, the RNAi mechanism can vary slightly between organisms [ 22 ]. In hemipterans, however, information on core RNAi-related genes is limited, and some studies report low RNAi efficiency because of the degradation of dsRNA by nucleases, dysfunctional RNAi machinery or unknown processing steps [ 23 ]. Despite the economic importance of M. fimbriolata , molecular studies of this species are currently lacking. The objective of this work was to characterize the M. fimbriolata transcriptome across different developmental stages to elucidate the molecular dynamics of its development, identify potential target genes for silencing, and investigate the presence and functionality of core RNAi machinery genes in this species.


## Materials and methods
Experimental design and RNA extraction M. fimbriolata specimens (Fig. 1 ) were collected from a Urochloa brizantha cv. Marandu field in Embrapa Pecuária Sudeste, São Carlos, Brazil (21°96′17″S and 47°84′21″W). Adults (males and females) and nymphs of all five instars (N1, N2, N3, N4, and N5) were collected and stored at −80 °C until RNA extraction. Eggs were maintained in the laboratory [ 24 ] and incubated in a BOD chamber under controlled conditions (25 °C, 60% relative humidity, 12 h light/dark cycle) until embryonic development was complete. Fig. 1 Representative images of the developmental stages of M. fimbriolata : second (N2), third (N3), fourth (N4) and fifth (N5) nymphal instars, and adult (AD). N1 (not in the figure), N2 and N3 were combined in “younger nymphs” (YN); N4 and N5 were combined in “older nymphs” (ON). Male and female adults were combined into the adult (AD) group Total RNA was extracted from first-, second-, and third-instar nymphs and pools of 100 eggs using a Quick-RNA Tissue/Insect Microprep Kit (Zymo Research, Irvine, USA). Owing to the greater tissue mass in fourth- and fifth-instar nymphs and wingless adults, these samples were ground with a mortar and pestle in liquid nitrogen, and total RNA was extracted using TRIzol reagent (Sigma–Aldrich, St. Louis, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. An optional centrifugation step after sample homogenization was performed before the addition of chloroform. The samples were precipitated with a 1:10 volume of 3 M sodium acetate, pH 5.5, followed by the addition of 500 µL of isopropanol and the Tri Reagent Solution Protocol. Three biological replicates per developmental stage yielded a total of 24 RNA samples. Individual whole nymphs and wingless adults were used for RNA extraction. RNA quantification and purity assessment were performed using a NanoDrop ND1000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., Waltham, USA). The extraction efficiency and RNA integrity were confirmed by agarose gel electrophoresis (1% agarose, ethidium bromide staining), and images of the gel were captured using a Molecular Imager Gel Doc XR system (Bio-Rad, Hercules, USA). RNA sequencing, data quality and filtering The samples were grouped into four developmental stages: eggs (EGs), younger nymphs (YNs: first, second and third instars), older nymphs (ONs: fourth and fifth instars), and adults (ADs: male and female). This grouping is supported by developmental physiology, as juvenile hormone (JH) levels remain high during early nymphal instars but start to decrease during the fourth instar [ 25 , 26 ]. Equal proportions of RNA mass extracted from the specimens at each stage were pooled, and cDNA libraries were constructed using a TruSeq Stranded mRNA Kit (Illumina, San Diego, USA), with three biological replicates per group, resulting in a total of 12 samples. Library quality was assessed on an Agilent 4200 TapeStation System (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, USA), and Illumina NextSeq 550 2 × 75 bp sequencing was performed at the Laboratório de Sequenciamento em Larga Escala (SELA), Faculdade de Medicina, Universidade de São Paulo (https://premium.fm.usp.br/index.php? mpg=11.42.00&lab=SELA). FastQC software version 0.11.9 [ 27 ] and MultiQC 1.11 [ 28 ] were used for quality control of the sequenced libraries. Adapters and low-quality reads were removed using Trimmomatic version 0.39 [ 29 ] with the Illumina Adapters and SlidingWindow 4:15 parameters. Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) was removed using SortMeRNA software version 4.3.6 [ 30 ] with default parameters. Transcriptome assembly and differential gene expression analysis Owing to the absence of a reference genome for M. fimbriolata , de novo transcriptome assembly was performed using Trinity version 2.15.1 [ 31 ], with the target K-mer coverage set to 50 (normalize_max_read_cov 50) and the other parameters set to the defaults. The longest isoform of each transcript (hereafter referred to as “Unigenes”) was selected using Trinity. Redundancies in the assembled sequences were removed using CD-HIT version 4.8.1 [ 32 ] with an 80% similarity threshold. Assembly quality and completeness were assessed using BUSCO version 5.5.0 [ 33 ] by comparing assembled transcripts to a set of conserved hemipterans orthologs (hemiptera_odb10 dataset). Read abundance was determined by mapping paired-end reads to the assembled transcriptomes using Bowtie2 version 2.4.1 [ 34 ]. Gene expression was quantified using Salmon software version 1.9.0 [ 35 ] and summarized via the tximport [ 36 ] package. Principal component analysis (PCA) [ 37 ] and hierarchical clustering based on Euclidean distance with Ward’s method of minimum variance [ 38 ] were performed to confirm the similarity between the replicates. Differential gene expression (DGE) analyses of the transcriptome data from specimens at different developmental stages (EGvsYN, EGvsON, EGvsAD, YNvsON, YNvsAD, ONvsAD) were performed using the edgeR package version 4.0.16 [ 39 ] implemented in R [ 40 ]. Raw gene quantification data were first normalized to a similar count across all samples from the same developmental stage and then to a minimum of 20 counts per million (CPM). For DGE comparisons, a false discovery rate (FDR)-corrected threshold of 0.01 and a minimum log 2 (fold change) (log 2 (FC)) of 2 were used. An intersection analysis was performed with UpSetR [ 41 ]. Functional annotation and enrichment TransDecoder software version 5.7.1 (Haas, B. J., https://github.com/TransDecoder/TransDecoder ) was used to predict open reading frames (ORFs) in the transcriptome and translate coding sequences into peptides. The functional annotation of the transcripts was performed using DIAMOND software version 2.1.8.162 [ 42 ] via searches against the SwissProt database (UniProt - https://www.uniprot.org/ ) and the nonredundant (NR) sequence database from NCBI, applying an e-value cutoff of 1e-5. Furthermore, protein domains were identified using HMMER software version 3.4 [ 43 ], and all the annotation results were integrated into a single report using Trinotate version 4.0.1 [ 44 ]. Gene Ontology (GO) terms [ 45 ] for functionally annotated sequences were retrieved, and differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in each comparison were analyzed for enrichment of biological processes via the topGO R package version 2.54.0 [ 46 ] with Fisher’s exact test ( p < 0.01). The enriched GO terms were summarized using REVIGO [ 47 ] with a 0.7 similarity threshold. Additionally, Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analysis [ 48 ] was performed with Drosophila melanogaster as a reference, with a significance threshold of p < 0.01. Gene coexpression network analysis (WGCNA) Weighted gene coexpression network analysis (WGCNA) was performed with the WGCNA R package [ 49 ] using the expression data remaining after edgeR’s preDEG filtering step and then transformed to transcripts per million (TPM) for the analysis. A β soft-threshold power was chosen on the basis of the lowest mean connectivity and corresponding to a linear regression coefficient (R²) of ~ 0.9. Plotting was performed with the igraph R package [ 50 ]. Modules with a minimum size of 30 genes were identified using a topological overlap matrix (TOM), and modules with coexpression similarities greater than 90% were merged. Hub genes were identified on the basis of the highest connectivity and betweenness measurements of each module. Modules were subjected to GO biological process enrichment analysis using topGO via a Fisher’s test with a threshold of 0.01 for significance and summarized via REVIGO [ 47 ] with a similarity threshold of 0.5 for visualization. Exploration of genes related to the RNAi machinery A list of 26 genes encoding proteins associated with the RNAi machinery was compiled from the literature [ 51 – 57 ], and corresponding genes were searched on the basis of the previous functional annotation of the M. fimbriolata transcriptome data: aubergine, Belle-DDX3, Clathrin, CLP1, DGCR8-Pasha, endoribonuclease Dicer-1, endoribonuclease Dicer-2, Epsin, HEN1, Innexin, protein argonaute 1, protein argonaute 2, protein argonaute 3, protein Gawky, protein Loquacious, protein Piwi, ribonuclease 3, RNA helicase armi, Scavenger, systemic RNA interference defective protein, SpindleE, Staufen, Translin, TRAX, Tudor, and Maelstrom. The sequences of six core RNAi-related genes, namely, DGCR8-Pasha, which is involved in the dsRNA binding pathway; endoribonuclease Dicer-1; ribonuclease 3, which is involved in the dsRNA cleavage pathway; Piwi-like protein Siwi; protein argonaute-2, which is involved in the endonuclease pathway; and RNA helicase Armi, which is associated with auxiliary RISC-associated or regulatory factors, were selected from the M. fimbriolata data. Homologs of these genes were retrieved from InsectBase 2.0 [ 58 ] for eight other insect species ( Apis mellifera , Bemisia tabaci , Bombyx mori , Drosophila melanogaster , Myzus persicae , Nilaparvata lugens , Tribolium castaneum , and Zootermopsis nevadensis ) and the springtail Folsomia candida (outgroup). For each gene, multiple sequence alignment was performed using ClustalW [ 59 ], and a phylogenetic tree was constructed using the maximum likelihood (ML) method in MEGAX [ 60 ]. Trees were generated using 1,000 bootstrap replicates, employing substitution models with the lowest Bayesian information criterion (BIC) score for each gene (Supplementary Table 1). Quantitative reverse transcription–PCR (RT–qPCR) The DGE analysis results and the expression of the core RNAi-related genes were validated by reverse transcription followed by quantitative PCR (RT–qPCR). Seven DEGs with high log 2 (FC) values were selected along with five core RNAi-related genes (Ago2, Gawky, Piwi, Scavenger, and SID). Four housekeeping genes were evaluated as potential endogenous controls: 18 S and 28 S ribosomal RNAs , selected on the basis of the literature [ 57 , 61 ], and alpha-(1,3)-fucosyltransferase 10 and adenine phosphoribosyltransferase, both of which exhibited stable expression across all samples in the M. fimbriolata transcriptome. The primer pairs (Supplementary Table 2) were designed using Primer3Input [ 62 ] and verified via NetPrimer (Premier Biosoft). The RNA samples were treated with deoxyribonuclease I (Invitrogen, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Inc., Waltham, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. cDNA synthesis was performed with the GoScript Reverse Transcription System A5000 (Promega, Madison, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. RT–qPCR was performed on a 7500 Real-Time PCR System (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., Waltham, USA) using the GoTaq qPCR Master Mix A6001 (Promega, Madison, USA) with the following cycling conditions: 95 °C for 120 s, followed by 40 cycles of 95 °C for 15 s and 60 s at 60 °C. Melting curves were obtained by heating from 60 to 95 °C to verify the products after amplification. All the qPCRs were performed using two technical and three biological replicates and analyzed via 7500 Software v2.3 (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., Waltham, USA). Relative gene expression was calculated using the ΔΔCt method [ 63 ], and statistical significance was assessed using the standard error of the mean (SEM).

## Results
Sequencing and quality control Sequencing of the RNA-seq library generated 586,279,569 paired-end reads, with an average of 48 million reads per library. Sequence quality analysis using FastQC software revealed that all 12 libraries presented similar quality profiles and revealed no major issues related to sequencing quality. After the reads were filtered using Trimmomatic, approximately 10.3% of the reads were discarded, resulting in 526,232,067 high-quality reads. Ribosomal RNA reads were removed with SortMeRNA software, reducing the number of reads by approximately 1.75%. In total, 517,345,920 reads remained for subsequent analysis. Transcriptome assembly and annotation A de novo transcriptome assembly performed at Trinity resulted in the identification of 434,018 transcripts. Given that this method is capable of generating several isoforms for the same putative gene, a file containing only the longest isoform for each gene was generated, resulting in a transcriptome comprising 309,002 unigenes. These unigenes underwent further redundancy removal on the basis of sequence similarity with CD-HIT, which resulted in the identification of 197,003 genes. General statistics were computed for each of these assemblies considering all the libraries together and are displayed in Table 1 . ## Sequencing and quality control
Sequencing of the RNA-seq library generated 586,279,569 paired-end reads, with an average of 48 million reads per library. Sequence quality analysis using FastQC software revealed that all 12 libraries presented similar quality profiles and revealed no major issues related to sequencing quality. After the reads were filtered using Trimmomatic, approximately 10.3% of the reads were discarded, resulting in 526,232,067 high-quality reads. Ribosomal RNA reads were removed with SortMeRNA software, reducing the number of reads by approximately 1.75%. In total, 517,345,920 reads remained for subsequent analysis.

A de novo transcriptome assembly performed at Trinity resulted in the identification of 434,018 transcripts. Given that this method is capable of generating several isoforms for the same putative gene, a file containing only the longest isoform for each gene was generated, resulting in a transcriptome comprising 309,002 unigenes. ## Discussion
Transcriptional and functional changes across development High-throughput transcriptome sequencing is a widely recognized method for studying functional genes [ 64 ]. In insects, it has been extensively used to explore potential pathways and genes associated with growth and development [ 65 – 67 ] and to identify potential RNAi targets for pest control. This approach provides insights into genes related to the RNAi machinery and facilitates target and housekeeping gene identification, even in species lacking a reference genome [ 57 , 68 , 69 ], such as Mahanarva fimbriolata . Spittlebugs are economically important xylem sap-sucking insects characterized by constant foam production [ 70 ]. However, this group is poorly characterized at the molecular level, with only one gene expression study available to date in Callitettix versicolor [ 71 ]. The M. fimbriolata de novo transcriptome assembly presented in this study is therefore a valuable resource. It yielded a high-quality reference with high completeness (96.6% from BUSCO analysis) and low duplication rates (5.3%). Despite these robust assembly metrics, the annotation rates in UniProt (8.25% of transcripts and 5.92% of proteins) and the NR database (19.85% and 13.12%, respectively) were relatively low, but an average of approximately 10,000 to 20,000 genes is estimated for insect genomes [ 58 ], according to the limited genomic and transcriptomic data for Cercopidae. This highlights the need for more data sequence information to improve the annotation accuracy for M. fimbriolata . The high number of DEGs observed between the M. fimbriolata developmental stages reflects substantial molecular changes, particularly between the eggs and other stages. This aligns with the metamorphosis process and is consistent with findings in holometabolous insects [ 72 – 74 ]. Furthermore, three RNAi machinery-related genes (Argonaute-2, Gawky, Scavenger, and SID) were differentially expressed between egg samples and other samples, suggesting greater RNAi activity in nymphs and adults. These results highlight the dynamic nature of gene expression during insect development and suggest potentially lower RNAi efficacy in eggs because of the reduced expression of RNAi-related proteins. Metamorphosis involves numerous biological changes, including changes in gene synthesis and expression, signaling pathways, hormone regulation, macromolecular substances and even long noncoding RNAs [ 75 , 76 ]. Hemimetabolous insects, which lack a pupal stage, develop from immature nymphs to reproductive adults through postembryonic modifications involving growth and morphological changes, such as ecdysis [ 77 ]. During each instar, the musculature is remodeled, adapting to the needs of the insect and supporting essential functions such as flight, reproduction and adult feeding [ 78 , 79 ]. Thus, the enriched GO terms identified in the DEGs in the nymph and adult samples (adult somatic muscle development, adult feeding behavior and flight) reflect complex tissue formation during development and its impact on gene expression in M. fimbriolata . In the egg samples, enrichment of the cell division GO term reflects its high activity following diapause termination, when this process is typically quiescent [ 80 , 81 ]. The enriched KEGG pathways in the egg samples, such as those involved in DNA replication and mismatch repair, as well as those involved in other arthropod studies [ 82 , 83 ], are also associated with rapid growth and preparation for embryogenesis. In contrast, the enriched GO terms in the nymph samples were related to fructose and mannose metabolism, which may be associated with body development [ 84 ]. Furthermore, energy metabolism pathways (glycolysis/gluconeogenesis) are crucial for sugar homeostasis and the production of trehalose, the primary hemolymph disaccharide in insects [ 85 , 86 ]. These essential pathways have already been targeted for RNAi-mediated gene silencing [ 87 – 89 ]. WGCNA reveals candidate genes for RNAi-based pest control These M. fimbriolata RNA-seq data provide a valuable resource for identifying potential RNAi targets. WGCNA enables candidate selection from DEG datasets [ 90 ]. In our spittlebug network, the hub gene in the largest module (Turquoise) is the DNA-directed RNA polymerase II subunit RPB7 (Fig. 5 ), an enzyme involved in transcription initiation and mRNA transport in insects [ 91 ] and a known RNAi target [ 92 , 93 ]. Another high-connectivity hub gene, Talin-2 (blue module), is a cytoskeleton protein important for muscle development and differentiation [ 94 , 95 ]. Talin-2 is highly expressed during Diaphorina citri nymphal development [ 96 ] and may represent a promising nymphal RNAi target. Other candidate genes in the pink module were highly expressed in adults and a (TRINITY_DN271358_c0_g1), a transcriptome dataset that remains unannotated despite manual homology searches, which showed only a low similarity to EVC ciliary complex subunit 1. The EVC protein plays a key role in regulating cell signaling and skeletal development in vertebrates [ 97 ]. Although no EVC orthologs have been reported in insects, its weak similarity may reflect a distant functional relationship, since the pink module was enriched for GO terms associated with hormone biosynthesis, including juvenile hormone (JH), estrogen, and steroid hormones. These hormones are crucial for insect metamorphosis: ecdysteroids trigger transition, whereas JH has an antimetamorphic effect, and its absence signals the induction of the next stage [ 98 , 99 ]. In adults, JH, acting as a gonadotropic hormone, is linked to reproduction and oviposition [ 100 , 101 ]. Silencing of JH-related genes can lead to abnormal phenotypes and increased mortality [ 102 ], suggesting the potential of these genes as RNAi targets. Further investigation of the function of this uncharacterized gene is warranted. Characterization and biological relevance of the core components of RNAi In the identification of RNAi machinery-related genes, highlighting the absence of homology to systemic RNA interference defective protein 2 (sid-2 ) in our data is important. Sid-2 -mediated dsRNA uptake in Caenorhabditis elegans [ 103 ] is uncommon in insects. Although not universally present in Hemiptera, sid-1 homologs have been identified in some species, such as Aphis glycines , Bemisia tabaci and Nilaparvata lugens [ 23 ], and are now also detected in M. fimbriolata . In N. lugens , functional studies have demonstrated that sid-1 is essential for systemic RNAi [ 104 ]. An alternative pathway, clathrin-dependent dsRNA uptake mediated by scavenger receptors, may contribute to uptake in species lacking sid-1 [ 105 ]. These observations suggest that RNAi uptake mechanisms in this insect group may involve high sid-1 systemic efficiency or other macromolecular transport proteins [ 106 ], but the efficiency and specific contribution of each mechanism are likely species specific [ 107 ]. In our analysis, RT–qPCR expression validation confirmed the presence of sid-1 and scavenger, but further functional bioassays are needed to confirm the complete RNAi pathway in this species. Phylogenetic analysis allowed us to infer the functions of some M. fimbriolata genes on the basis of their sequence similarity to those of other insect species. For example, Dicer1 , which is crucial for the maturation of miRNAs from pre-miRNAs [ 108 , 109 ], is generally conserved in animals [ 110 ]. In N. lugens , the closest relative of M. fimbriolata in the Dicer1 phylogenetic tree, low Dicer1 expression impairs miRNA processing, affecting reproductive development and oocyte formation [ 104 , 111 ]. Given that N. lugens is a model species for hemimetabolous insect RNAi [ 112 ], similar Dicer1 functions can be expected in M. fimbriolata . Similarly, a phylogenetic analysis of the DGCR8-Pasha gene, which is responsible for generating a precursor microRNA [ 113 ], has provided insight into the potential functions of this gene in Myzus cerasi and Bemisia tabaci , two Hemiptera species that have been extensively investigated in RNAi studies [ 23 , 114 , 115 ]. The close similarity between our spittlebug species and Apis mellifera in terms of ribonuclease III, an enzyme that initiates microRNA processing [ 116 ], highlights the need for caution when designing RNAi-based approaches to avoid unintended effects on pollinators. An insect’s developmental stage critically influences RNAi, with gene silencing often stronger in eggs and nymphs, despite adults being easier to handle [ 117 ]. Although some RNAi machinery genes (e.g., Ago2 and Gawky ) are more highly expressed in later developmental stages of M. fimbriolata than in eggs, they are still present. This lower expression in eggs is consistent with their primary roles, with Ago2 being associated with cellular morphogenesis, molting, and new cuticle formation [ 118 ], and Gawky being involved in ovarian development and reproduction [ 119 ]. In contrast, Piwi is predominantly expressed in eggs, as expected given that piRNAs are involved in germline development and stem cell renewal [ 120 ]. The reduced RNAi efficiency in adults may result not from an inactive machinery but from the targeting of highly expressed genes, which limits silencing effectiveness [ 121 ]. Thus, careful selection of target genes is essential to ensure effective and consistent RNAi responses in insects.

The enriched KEGG pathways in the egg samples, such as those involved in DNA replication and mismatch repair, as well as those involved in other arthropod studies [ 82 , 83 ], are also associated with rapid growth and preparation for embryogenesis. In contrast, the enriched GO terms in the nymph samples were related to fructose and mannose metabolism, which may be associated with body development [ 84 ]. Furthermore, energy metabolism pathways (glycolysis/gluconeogenesis) are crucial for sugar homeostasis and the production of trehalose, the primary hemolymph disaccharide in insects [ 85 , 86 ]. These essential pathways have already been targeted for RNAi-mediated gene silencing [ 87 – 89 ].


## WGCNA reveals candidate genes for RNAi-based pest control
These M. fimbriolata RNA-seq data provide a valuable resource for identifying potential RNAi targets. WGCNA enables candidate selection from DEG datasets [ 90 ]. In our spittlebug network, the hub gene in the largest module (Turquoise) is the DNA-directed RNA polymerase II subunit RPB7 (Fig. 5 ), an enzyme involved in transcription initiation and mRNA transport in insects [ 91 ] and a known RNAi target [ 92 , 93 ]. Another high-connectivity hub gene, Talin-2 (blue module), is a cytoskeleton protein important for muscle development and differentiation [ 94 , 95 ]. Talin-2 is highly expressed during Diaphorina citri nymphal development [ 96 ] and may represent a promising nymphal RNAi target. Other candidate genes in the pink module were highly expressed in adults and a (TRINITY_DN271358_c0_g1), a transcriptome dataset that remains unannotated despite manual homology searches, which showed only a low similarity to EVC ciliary complex subunit 1. The EVC protein plays a key role in regulating cell signaling and skeletal development in vertebrates [ 97 ]. Although no EVC orthologs have been reported in insects, its weak similarity may reflect a distant functional relationship, since the pink module was enriched for GO terms associated with hormone biosynthesis, including juvenile hormone (JH), estrogen, and steroid hormones. These hormones are crucial for insect metamorphosis: ecdysteroids trigger transition, whereas JH has an antimetamorphic effect, and its absence signals the induction of the next stage [ 98 , 99 ]. In adults, JH, acting as a gonadotropic hormone, is linked to reproduction and oviposition [ 100 , 101 ]. Silencing of JH-related genes can lead to abnormal phenotypes and increased mortality [ 102 ], suggesting the potential of these genes as RNAi targets. Further investigation of the function of this uncharacterized gene is warranted.


## Characterization and biological relevance of the core components of RNAi
In the identification of RNAi machinery-related genes, highlighting the absence of homology to systemic RNA interference defective protein 2 (sid-2 ) in our data is important. Sid-2 -mediated dsRNA uptake in Caenorhabditis elegans [ 103 ] is uncommon in insects. Although not universally present in Hemiptera, sid-1 homologs have been identified in some species, such as Aphis glycines , Bemisia tabaci and Nilaparvata lugens [ 23 ], and are now also detected in M. fimbriolata . In N. lugens , functional studies have demonstrated that sid-1 is essential for systemic RNAi [ 104 ]. An alternative pathway, clathrin-dependent dsRNA uptake mediated by scavenger receptors, may contribute to uptake in species lacking sid-1 [ 105 ]. These observations suggest that RNAi uptake mechanisms in this insect group may involve high sid-1 systemic efficiency or other macromolecular transport proteins [ 106 ], but the efficiency and specific contribution of each mechanism are likely species specific [ 107 ]. In our analysis, RT–qPCR expression validation confirmed the presence of sid-1 and scavenger, but further functional bioassays are needed to confirm the complete RNAi pathway in this species. Phylogenetic analysis allowed us to infer the functions of some M. fimbriolata genes on the basis of their sequence similarity to those of other insect species. For example, Dicer1 , which is crucial for the maturation of miRNAs from pre-miRNAs [ 108 , 109 ], is generally conserved in animals [ 110 ]. In N. lugens , the closest relative of M. fimbriolata in the Dicer1 phylogenetic tree, low Dicer1 expression impairs miRNA processing, affecting reproductive development and oocyte formation [ 104 , 111 ]. Given that N. lugens is a model species for hemimetabolous insect RNAi [ 112 ], similar Dicer1 functions can be expected in M. fimbriolata . Similarly, a phylogenetic analysis of the DGCR8-Pasha gene, which is responsible for generating a precursor microRNA [ 113 ], has provided insight into the potential functions of this gene in Myzus cerasi and Bemisia tabaci , two Hemiptera species that have been extensively investigated in RNAi studies [ 23 , 114 , 115 ]. The close similarity between our spittlebug species and Apis mellifera in terms of ribonuclease III, an enzyme that initiates microRNA processing [ 116 ], highlights the need for caution when designing RNAi-based approaches to avoid unintended effects on pollinators. An insect’s developmental stage critically influences RNAi, with gene silencing often stronger in eggs and nymphs, despite adults being easier to handle [ 117 ]. Although some RNAi machinery genes (e.g., Ago2 and Gawky ) are more highly expressed in later developmental stages of M. fimbriolata than in eggs, they are still present. This lower expression in eggs is consistent with their primary roles, with Ago2 being associated with cellular morphogenesis, molting, and new cuticle formation [ 118 ], and Gawky being involved in ovarian development and reproduction [ 119 ]. In contrast, Piwi is predominantly expressed in eggs, as expected given that piRNAs are involved in germline development and stem cell renewal [ 120 ]. The reduced RNAi efficiency in adults may result not from an inactive machinery but from the targeting of highly expressed genes, which limits silencing effectiveness [ 121 ]. Thus, careful selection of target genes is essential to ensure effective and consistent RNAi responses in insects.


## Conclusion
In this study, we successfully characterized the transcriptome of Mahanarva fimbriolata across different developmental stages, providing insights into the molecular mechanisms involved in this process. The results revealed significant molecular changes throughout the development of M. fimbriolata , emphasizing the dynamic regulation of gene expression between the egg stage and other stages. Our findings also allowed us to identify RNAi machinery-related genes and explore potential targets for silencing, in addition to inferring the functions of some of these genes on the basis of their functions in other insect species. The RNAi target genes identified in this study provide opportunities for future pest control strategies for this species and may serve as potential candidates for similar efforts in other hemiptera species. These results not only expand the molecular knowledge of M. fimbriolata but also provide new perspectives for future research focused on potential biotechnological applications.
