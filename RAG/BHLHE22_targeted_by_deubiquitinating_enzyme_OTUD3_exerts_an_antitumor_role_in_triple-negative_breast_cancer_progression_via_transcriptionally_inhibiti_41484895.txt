TITLE: BHLHE22, targeted by deubiquitinating enzyme OTUD3, exerts an antitumor role in triple-negative breast cancer progression via transcriptionally inhibiting CDT1
PMID: 41484895
PMC: 
------------------------------------------------------------

# ABSTRACT
Background Basic helix-loop-helix family, member e22 (BHLHE22), a basic helix loop helix transcription factor family member, functions vary in different types of cancer. Currently, its function in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is unclear. Methods The GSE45827 and GSE113865 datasets were used to screen potential TNBC marker. Biological websites were used to analyze BHLHE22 expression in TNBC, its relationship with the prognosis of patients with TNBC, and its potential function. A series of in vitro and in vivo experiments was performed to investigate the function of BHLHE22 in TNBC. The regulatory relationship between OTU domain-containing protein 3 (OTUD3) and BHLHE22 was verified by Co-Immunoprecipitation, ubiquitination assay, and site-specific mutation experiments. The mRNA-seq analysis was performed to identify potential genes for the anti-cancer role of BHLHE22. The transcriptional regulation of DNA replication factor 1 (CDT1) by BHLHE22 was confirmed by dual luciferase reporter assay. Results We identified the downregulation of BHLHE22 in TNBC tissues based on the GSE45827 and GSE113865 datasets. The expression of BHLHE22 in TBNC patients was lower than that in non-TNBC patients, and patients at stages 3 and 4 tended to express lower BHLHE22 expression than patients at stages 1 and 2. Patients with high expression of BHLHE22 had better survival prognosis than those with BHLHE22 low expression. Functional studies revealed that BHLHE22 overexpression impaired cell growth in vitro and in vivo . However, BHLHE22 silencing enhanced the malignant behaviors of cancer cells. OTUD3, a deubiquitinase known to suppress TNBC progression, was found to increase the stability of BHLHE22 protein through deubiquitination regulation. The C76 site mutation of OTUD3 eliminated the catalytic activity of OTUD3 and failed to regulate the protein stability of BHLHE22. Furthermore, BHLHE22 medicated the antitumor effect of OTUD3 in TNBC. The mRNA-seq analysis identified potential genes for the anti-cancer role of BHLHE22, involving CDT1. BHLHE22 was proved to reduce CDT1 RNA expression by blocking CDT1 transcription. The anti-proliferative effect of BHLHE22 overexpression was reversed by CDT1 overexpression. Conclusions Our observations demonstrate that BHLHE22 may be a promising target for TNBC therapy. # BODY
## Introduction
Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is a subtype of breast cancer characterized by the lack of expression of estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) [ 1 ]. Accounting for 15–20% of all breast cancer cases, TNBC is notably more aggressive, with higher recurrence and mortality rates compared to other subtypes [ 2 ]. Since TNBC patients are ineligible for endocrine therapy or HER2-targeted treatments, chemotherapy and radiotherapy remain the cornerstone of systemic therapy [ 3 ]. Elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying TNBC progression is critical for developing more effective therapeutic strategies. Basic helix-loop-helix family, member e22 (BHLHE22), a member of the spiral ring transcription factor family, plays a role in cell differentiation during neuronal development [ 4 ]. Emerging evidence suggests that BHLHE22 may be involved in cancer progression and its function varies with different types of cancer. For instance, in prostate cancer, BHLHE22 promoted the formation of an immunosuppressive bone tumor microenvironment by recruiting immunosuppressive neutrophils and monocytes, thereby facilitating bone metastasis [ 5 ]. Conversely, in endometrial cancer, BHLHE22 expression was downregulated, and its high expression was correlated with improved patient survival by suppressing tumor cell proliferation and metastasis [ 6 ]. At present, the function of BHLHE22 in TNBC is unknown. Analysis of The Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) datasets and online resources revealed that BHLHE22 was downregulated in TNBC tissues and negatively associated with poor patient prognosis, prompting further investigation into its potential tumor-suppressive mechanisms in TNBC. Protein post-translational modification is the main mechanism of protein function regulation and plays an important role in regulating various cellular physiological and pathological processes [ 7 ]. The upstream mechanisms that regulate post-translational modification of BHLHE22 was thus explored. The protein interacting with BHLHE22 was queried through the HitPredict website ( https://www.hitpredict.org/ ), and OTU domain-containing protein 3 (OTUD3) was found to interact with BHLHE22. OTUD3 is a deubiquitinase and can increase protein stability by removing substrate ubiquitination. By increasing the protein stability of p53 in DU4475 cells, OTUD3 played an anti-proliferative role in TNBC [ 8 ]. Based on this, we speculate that OTUD3 may affect the ubiquitination modification of BHLHE22. As a member of the spiral transcription factor family, BHLHE22 can not only inhibit gene transcription, but also promote gene transcription [ 5 , 9 ]. The mRNA-seq was performed at the cellular level to explore the potential mechanism by which BHLHE22 influenced TNBC progression. Based on the sequencing results, we focused on chromatin licensing and DNA replication factor 1 (CDT1), which was significantly down-regulated in BHLHE22-overexpressed cells. CDT1 is an important regulator of DNA replication initiation and is essential for loading the microchromosome maintenance complex helicase to the origin of DNA replication [ 10 ]. High expression of CDT1 was associated with poor prognosis in TNBC patients, and overexpression of CDT1 promoted cell proliferation [ 11 ]. The binding of BHLHE22 to the promoter region of CDT1 was predicted by the Jaspar website ( https://jaspar.elixir.no/analysis ). Thus, transcriptional inhibition of CDT1 may be a potential mechanism for BHLHE22 to play an anti-proliferative role in TNBC.


## Materials and methods
TNBC database mining The transcriptome and sample information were downloaded for GSE113865 and GSE45827 from The Gene Expression Omnibus. There were 3 TNBC and 3 normal tissues in GSE113865 . GSE45827 contained 41 tumor tissues and 11 normal tissues. Genes with |log2(fold change)|> 1 and p value < 0.05 were considered differentially expressed genes (DEGs). DEGs in each dataset underwent bioinformatics analysis. Tissue samples TNBC paraffin tissue sections from 51 patients were collected from the Liaoning Cancer Hospital. The informed consents were obtained from all participants. All experiments were approved by the Ethics Committee of Liaoning Cancer Hospital. Cell culture Human TNBC cell lines MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-157 were purchased from iCell (China). The cell lines were cultured in L15 medium (cat. LA9510, Solarbio, China) mixed with fetal bovine serum (FBS, 10%). 293 T cells, obtained from iCell, was cultured in DMEM medium (cat. G4511, Servicebio, China) containing 10% FBS. All medium used in the study were maintained in a 37℃ incubator with 5% CO 2 . Lentiviral vector infection The inducible expression pLVX-TetOne-Puro vector was used to overexpress BHLHE22, Tet-pLKO-puro vector was used to silence BHLHE22. After cell infection, the cells were cultured in complete medium containing puromycin for 5 d and the medium was renewed every 2 d. Then, the antibiotic screening was removed and the cells were cultured in complete media for about 10 d until multiple monoclonal cell clusters were visible to the naked eye. The monoclonal cell cluster continued to be passaged and cultured, ultimately obtaining a stable infected cell line. The infected cells were treated with doxycycline (dox, 1 μg/mL) to induce BHLHE22 overexpression or silencing. Quantitative real-time PCR (RT-qPCR) Total RNA was extracted from cells using TRIpure lysate (cat. RP1001, BioTeke, China). NanoDrop One ultraviolet spectrophotometer (Thermo, USA) was used to determine RNA concentration. Total RNA (1.0 μg) was reverse transcribed into cDNA using All-in-One First-Strand SuperMix (cat. MD80101, Magen, China). RNA expression was measured by qPCR using the 2 × Taq PCR MasterMix (cat. PC1150, Solarbio, China) and SYBR Green (cat. SR4110, Solarbio). β-actin acted as an internal control. The primer sequences for qPCR were: forward (5’-3’) TGCGGAGCGTCTTTGTG; reverse (5’-3’) CAGTGATGTGGGCGAGGT (CDT1). forward (5’-3’) AGAAATCCAAAGAGCAAAAGG; reverse (5’-3’) ATGTAGTTCTTGGCGAGCAG (BHLHE22); forward (5’-3’) GGCACCCAGCACAATGAA, reverse (5’-3’) TAGAAGCATTTGCGGTGG (β-actin). MTT assay Cells in different groups were seeded to 96-well plates (4 × 10 3 cells/well). On the next day, MTT solution (50 μL; cat. KGA9301, KeyGEN, China) was added to each well at indicated time. Following an incubation for 4 h, purple crystals that formed were dissolved in 150 μL DMSO (cat. KGH2110, KeyGEN). The absorbance of the plate was obtained with a microplate reader (cat. 800TS, BioTEK, USA) at 490 nm. Flow cytometry analysis of DNA replication DNA replication in the cells was detected using the EdU cell proliferation assay kit (cat. E-CK-A370, Elabscience, China). Different groups of cells were inoculated into the 6-well plates with 2 × 10 5 cells each well. The cells were re-suspended with 1 mL PBS solution containing 1% bovine serum albumin (BSA) and treated with 4% formaldehyde for 15 min away from light. After centrifugation, the supernatant was discarded and 1 mL PBS solution containing 1% BSA was added to re-suspend the cells. After the supernatant was discarded, the cells were re-suspended in 0.5 mL PBS solution containing 1% Saponin and incubated at room temperature for 20 min and then incubated with Click reaction solution for 30 min in the dark. After incubating with 1% Saponin for 5 min, the percentage of EdU-positive cells was detected by flow cytometry. Colony formation assay The cells of each group were inoculated in the petri dish by 300 cells/dish with 1 μg/mL dox. Two weeks later, cells were fixed with paraformaldehyde (4%) for 25 min at room temperature and stained with crystal violet (cat. 0528, Amresco, USA). Clone formation rate = (number of clones/number of inoculated cells) × 100%. Apoptosis detection To analyze apoptosis, cells were seeded in a 6-well plate and apoptosis was detected by using the cell apoptosis detection kit (cat. KGA1102, KeyGEN). Briefly, the cells were washed twice with PBS and then re-suspended with 500 μL Binding Buffer. After incubating with AnnexinV-FITC (5 μL) and Propidium Iodide (PI, 5 μL) for 15 min in the dark, cell apoptosis was analyzed using a flow cytometry. The activities of caspase 3 and 9 were measured by using caspase-3/9 activity detection kit (cat. C1116/C1158, Beyotime, China). Tumor xenograft assay Female nude mice (6 weeks old; BALB/c nude) were purchased from Huachuang Xinuo Medical Technology Co., LTD (China) and maintained in pathogen-free conditions. The 5 × 10 6 MDA-MB-157 cells with BHLHE22 silencing or overexpression in logarithmic growth phase were injected subcutaneously into nude mice. After the tumor became visible to the naked eye (day 10), the mice were fed drinking water containing dox (1 mg/mL). The tumor volume was recorded every 5 days for 35 days. On the last day, nude mice were photographed, tumor body was stripped and the changes in tumor volume and weight were analyzed. Then, tumor tissues were fixed with paraformaldehyde for immunohistochemical staining. Immunohistochemical staining The tumor tissues were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, dehydrated, dewaxed, embedded, and sliced into 5 μm sections. The sections were then incubated in a boiling antigen-repair solution for 10 min and quenched for endogenous peroxidase activity with H 2 O 2 (0.3%) solution for 15 min. After blocking with 1% BSA (cat. A602440-0050, Sangon, China) for 15 min at room temperature, the sections were incubated with the primary antibody against BHLHE22 (1:100 dilution, cat. 25622–1-AP, Proteintech, China) and Ki67 (1:100 dilution, cat. AF0198, Affinity, China) at 4℃ overnight. Next day, the slides were treated with the goat anti-mouse or rabbit secondary antibody (1:200 dilution, cat. D110087/D110058, Sangon) for 60 min. Sections were visualized by 3,3’-diaminobenzidine (DAB) treating and counterstained by hematoxylin. Images were obtained using a microscope (cat. BX53, Olympus, Japan). The analysis of the expression level of BHLHE22 in cancer tissues is similar to previous study [ 12 ]. Number of BHLHE22 positive tumor cells/number of total tumor cells > 10% was counted as positive ( +), < 10% was regarded as negative (-). Western blot, Co-Immunoprecipitation (Co-IP), and ubiquitination assay analysis Total protein in cells was extracted and the protein concentration was examined using a BCA protein assay kit. The same amount protein (20 µg/lane) was subjected to sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis and the separated proteins were transferred to polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) membrane. The membranes were then treated with primary antibody overnight at 4℃ followed by the incubation of secondary antibody for 2 h. The protein bands on the membranes were visualized by treating with ECL solution. β-actin acted as internal control. A target protein-specific anti-Flag/BHLHE22 or anti-His/OTUD3 antibody was crosslinked with AminoLink coupling resin for 120 min. The resin-antibody complexes were suspended with protein lysate. The resin-antibody complexes were then washed with extraction buffer, and the immunoprecipitants were collected by centrifugation and subjected to western blot analysis. The ubiquitination assay was performed to analyzed the ubiquitin modification of proteins. His-tagged BHLHE22 vector, flag-tagged OTUD3 vector, and HA-tagged ubiquitin (ubi) vector were co-transfected into 293 T cells, 48 h later, cells were treated with MG132 (10 μM) for 6 h, CO-IP assay was used to detected the ubiquitination of BHLHE22. Flag–BHLHE22 protein purified from the 293 T cells were eluted in SDS-sample buffer and immunoblotted with anti-HA antibody and anti-Flag antibody. The information of antibodies was shown in Supplementary Table 1 . RNA extraction and library preparation Cell lysis was performed using Trizol solution (10 cm 2 /mL/5 × 10 6 cells) and total RNA was extracted with the help of chloroform and ethanol. The mRNA with poly(A) was enriched using Oligo(dT) magnetic beads and fragmented using divalent cations. Using fragmented mRNA as template and random oligonucleotide as primer, the first strand of cDNA was synthesized in M-MuLV reverse transcriptase system. The second strand of cDNA was synthesized by using dNTPs and DNA polymerase I. The purified double-stranded cDNA was end-repaired, A tail was added and a sequencing connector was connected. AMPure XP beads were used to screen cDNA about 370 ~ 420 bp for PCR amplification and the PCR products were purified again using AMPure XP beads to obtain the final library. After the library was constructed, initial quantification was performed using Qubit2.0 Fluorometer and diluted to 1.5 ng/uL. The insert size of the library was detected using Agilent 2100 bioanalyzer. RT-qPCR was used to accurately quantify the effective concentration of the library. Sequencing and RNA-seq analysis The sequencing images were generated by the sequencing platform and converted into sequential data (reads) by CASAVA base recognition. The fastq file contains the sequence information of sequencing fragments and their corresponding sequencing quality information. The raw data from sequencing contained a small number of reads with sequencing connectors or with low sequencing quality. In order to ensure the quality and reliability of data analysis, the original data were filtered. After filtering out the joints and low-quality reads, clean reads were aligned to the human genome (GRCh38) using HISAT2 software. HTSeq (v2.0.2) transcriptome analysis quantitative software was used for the reads count. The FPKM of each gene was then calculated based on the length of the gene and the readings mapped to that gene were calculated. DESeq2 software was used to perform differential expression analysis between different groups. P < 0.001 & |log2 (foldchange)|≥ 2 was set as the threshold for significant differential expression. The cluster Profiler software was used for Gene Ontology (GO) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) enrichment analysis of DEGs. Dual luciferase reporter assay The plasmids involved in the dual-luciferase assay in this study were designed and synthesized by General Biosystems Inc. (China). Promoter constructs for the assays were obtained by cloning the region of the human CDT1 promoter (from − 2000 bp to + 50 bp, from − 1500 bp to + 50 bp, from − 1000 bp to + 50 bp, from − 500 bp to + 50 bp). ## Results
Interest gene screening To screen potential marker genes in TNBC, gene expression profiles from GSE45827 (41 tumor tissues and 11 normal tissues) and GSE113865 (3 tumor tissues and 3 normal tissues) datasets were analyzed. The gene co-expression network of TNBC based on GSE45827 was constructed using WGCNA (Fig. 1 a). We then constructed a hierarchical clustering tree that constituted a gene module. This branch showed several genes with similar expression profiles. Each gene represented a leaf on a tree. In addition, GSE45827 was built with 4 modules (Fig. 1 b). We selected turquoise and blue modules with the highest correlation coefficients as candidate hub modules for the GSE45827 dataset (Fig. 1 c). The scatter plot showed the expression of genes in the turquoise and blue modules in normal and cancer tissues (Fig. 1 c). Venn Diagram identified the overlapping genes in turquoise and blue modules and GSE113865 dataset and 824 genes were obtained (Fig. 1 d). The volcano map showed the expression of these overlapping genes (Fig. 1 e). Transcription factors (TFs) are known to regulate the transcription of downstream genes by binding to the promoter region of downstream genes, and studies have shown that targeted suppression of TFs can improve the prognosis of BC [ 13 ]. We therefore focused on the functional study of TFs in TNBC progression. We obtained a list of TFs from the AnimalTFDB website ( https://guolab.wchscu.cn/AnimalTFDB4/ /#/ ). The 51 differentially expressed TFs were identified by intersecting 824 overlapping genes (yellow) with TFs (cyan, Fig. 1 f). From these 51 TFs, the functions of 43 TFs are already known in BC (black color), 5 TFs (red color) lack evidence of involvement in cancer progression, and the three candidate genes (green color, MESP1, BHLHE22, and BHLHE41) were obtained. We note that both BHLHE22 and BHLHE41 belong to the basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) family of transcription factors, and those family members are widely expressed transcription factors involved in the progression of various cancers [ 14 ]. Based on this, we prioritized investigating these family members, BHLHE22 and BHLHE41. We found that BHLHE22 and basic helix-loop-helix transcription factor 41 (BHLHE41) were significantly down-regulated in the GSE113865 and GSE45827 datasets (Fig. 1 g). ## The OTU domain of OTUD3 mediates the deubiquitination of BHLHE22
It has been reported that the mutation of cysteine at position 76 to alanine deactivates the catalytic activity [ 16 , 17 ]. The next experiments were performed to explore whether this site affected the protein stability of BHLHE22. In the presence of C76 mutation, protein interaction between OTUD3 and BHLHE22 was still detected, but it resulted in increased ubiquitination of BHLHE22 (Fig. 6 i and j). ## Discussion
After a series of bioinformatics analysis, BHLHE22 was focused and might be a potential biomarker for the development of TNBC. BHLHE22 expression was significantly decreased in tumor tissues and its low expression was associated with poor prognosis in BC patients. The anti-proliferative effect of BHLHE22 was observed in vivo and in vitro . Through mRNA-seq sequencing, we identified the potential mechanism by which BHLHE22 played an anti-proliferative role, in part through transcriptional inhibition of CDT1 expression. Further, the protein stability of BHLHE22 was enhanced by OTUD3 and mediated the anticancer effect of OTUD3 in TNBC. Collectively these data demonstrated a novel antitumor role for BHLHE22 in TNBC. Basic helix loop helix family members are widely expressed transcription factor families, and most members of this family contain the bHLH domain, which is mainly responsible for DNA binding [ 18 ]. Due to the nature of their transcription factors, members of this family are involved in the progression of various diseases, including cancer [ 19 ]. And different members have different functions in cancer. For example, BHLHE40 played a role in promoting survival and metastasis in the BC process rather than BHLHE41 [ 19 , 20 ]. Low expression of BHLHE41 was associated with poor prognosis of BC patients [ 21 ]. Currently, the function of BHLHE22 in TNBC is unknown. This study identified the anti-proliferative effect of BHLHE22 in TNBC and was associated with a good prognosis in patients with BC, which is similar to the function previously reported in endometrial cancer [ 6 ]. Genetic mutations are often found in the course of cancer, and mutations in some genes may alter their function such as p53 [ 22 ]. BHLHE22 was not mutated in TNBC, suggesting that the alteration of BHLHE22 expression may be the main reason affecting TNBC progression. Although the current data only demonstrates low expression of BHLHE22 at the RNA level in TNBC without detecting its protein expression, the clinical correlation analysis further proves its tumor-suppressive role in TNBC. The 26S proteasome is an adenosine triphosphate dependent multicatalytic protease responsible for most non-lysozymic intracellular protein degradation [ 23 ]. Proteasomes are critical for controlling many cellular processes, including transcription factors, cell cycle progression, and apoptosis [ 24 ]. We obtained two ubiquitin-regulating enzymes that interact with BHLHE22. OTUD3 is a deubiquitinating enzyme known to inhibit the growth of TNBC cells by increasing the protein stability of p53 [ 8 ]. Parkin (PAKR2) is a ubiquitinating enzyme, and its role in TNBC is unknown. Based on this, we preferentially chose OTUD3 with clear functions to study. OTUD3 consists of a tumor domain-containing (OTU) domain and a ubiquitin associated (UBA) domain, and the OTU domain is the catalytic domain that is key for OTUD3 to perform deubiquitination activity and interact with the substrate. The mutation of C76 eliminated the catalytic activity of OTUD3 but did not affect its binding to the substrate BHLHE22, and the results is consistent with previous reports [ 16 ]. Since the biological function of OTUD3 is mainly achieved by affecting the stability of downstream proteins, the elimination of OTUD3 catalytic activity restored the effects caused by OTUD3 overexpression, indicating that the effects of OTUD3 on tumor growth is indispensable on its catalytic activity, which was observed in lung cancer cells [ 17 ]. Changes in protein levels arise not only from transcriptional regulation but also from post-translational modifications. The dysregulation of BHLHE22 at the RNA level may involve regulation by certain miRNAs, such as miR-24 [ 25 , 26 ], which will be investigated in future research. The potential mechanism and related functions of BHLHE22 in exerting anti-tumor effects in TNBC were analyzed through high-throughput omics. DEGs were mainly annotated in the aspects of cell proliferation, cell adhesion, and angiogenesis, etc., which are important phenotypes of cancer progression. The annotation of cell proliferation is consistent with the results of our database analysis, suggesting a close relationship between BHLHE22 and cancer cell growth. However, the functional enrichment results indicate which biological processes BHLHE22 is associated with, but cannot determine whether it acts as a positive or negative regulator. A series of experiments further demonstrated that BHLHE22 plays an anti-apoptotic and proliferation-promoting role in the progression of TNBC. In addition to the enrichment of proliferation, these DEGs were also enriched into T cell differentiation pathways. CD4 + T cell contain two subpopulations, helper T (Th)1 and Th2 cells, and produce different cytokines in BC [ 27 ]. Th1 cells produce tumor necrosis factor α, interferon gamma, interleukin (IL)-2, and IL-12, which mediate antitumor effects. Th2 cells produce IL-4 and IL-10 and promote tumor growth by suppressing the host immune system [ 27 ]. Our results demonstrate that BHLHE22 may also play a role in the tumor immune microenvironment such as T cell differentiation, and we will explore this issue in future studies. Based on this property of cell growth, we found six candidate genes through functional enrichment. CDT1 is a protein of the prereplication complex that is critical for loading microchromosome maintenance complex helicase onto the DNA replication starting point [ 10 , 28 ]. CDT1 increased tumorigenesis by driving overreplication, such as in colorectal cancer and TNBC [ 11 , 29 ]. In addition, studies have shown that CDC6 is highly expressed in BC tissues and is associated with poor prognosis in BC patients, suggesting a cancer-promoting role of CDC6 in BC [ 30 ]. Our results showed that overexpression of BHLHE22 significantly reduced the expression of CDC6 in TNBC cells, suggesting that CDC6 may also be one of the potential mechanisms by which BHLHE22 exerts a cancer-inhibiting effect on TNBC. Other factors were not reported in TNBC. Furthermore, we demonstrate that BHLHE22 inhibit cell metastasis. The inhibitory effect of BHLHE22 on cell metastasis has also been observed in endometrial cancer cells [ 6 ]. Previous studies demonstrated that CDT1 and CDC45 plays a role in promoting metastasis and proliferation in cancers [ 11 , 31 ]. We hypothesize that the anti-metastasis effect of BHLHE22 may be achieved in part by modulating CDT1 and CDC45, which is our next research direction.


## Conclusions
Therefore, this study identified BHLHE22 as a promising potential target for TNBC through database screening. BHLHE22 played an antitumor role in TNBC by influencing transcription of downstream factors, such as CDT1. Meanwhile, post-translational modification of BHLHE22 was regulated by the deubiquitinating enzyme OTUD3. Our results suggest that BHLHE22 may be a promising target for TNBC therapy.
